# Story Structure

A reference guide to the major structural frameworks used in fiction writing. No single framework is "correct" -- different stories benefit from different architectures. The goal is always the same: create a narrative experience that feels both surprising and inevitable.

---

## Three-Act Structure

The most widely used framework in Western storytelling. Aristotle described it; Hollywood codified it.

**Act I -- Setup (25%)**
- Establish the ordinary world and the protagonist's status quo
- Introduce the central dramatic question
- Inciting incident disrupts the status quo (around the 10-15% mark)
- First plot point / turning point propels the protagonist into Act II

**Act II -- Confrontation (50%)**
- Rising action: obstacles, complications, false victories
- Midpoint reversal (50%): a revelation or event that shifts the protagonist's understanding
- Second plot point / turning point (75%): the "all is lost" moment or dark night of the soul
- The protagonist's old approach fails completely; a new approach is forced

**Act III -- Resolution (25%)**
- Climax: the protagonist faces the central conflict using what they've learned
- The dramatic question is answered
- Falling action: the aftermath and new status quo
- Resolution: the protagonist has changed (or definitively failed to change)

**When to use it:** First novels, genre fiction, screenplays, any story where you want clear forward momentum.

## The Hero's Journey (Joseph Campbell / Christopher Vogler)

A myth-based structure that maps the protagonist's transformation through a cycle of departure, initiation, and return.

1. **Ordinary World** -- The hero's normal life before the adventure
2. **Call to Adventure** -- The challenge or quest is presented
3. **Refusal of the Call** -- Hesitation, fear, or obligation holds the hero back
4. **Meeting the Mentor** -- A guide appears with wisdom or tools
5. **Crossing the Threshold** -- The hero commits to the journey, leaving the ordinary world
6. **Tests, Allies, Enemies** -- The hero learns the rules of the special world
7. **Approach to the Inmost Cave** -- Preparation for the major ordeal
8. **The Ordeal** -- The hero faces their greatest fear or most dangerous foe
9. **Reward** -- The hero claims what they came for (a sword, an elixir, knowledge)
10. **The Road Back** -- The hero must return to the ordinary world, often chased
11. **Resurrection** -- A final test where the hero applies everything learned
12. **Return with the Elixir** -- The hero returns transformed, bearing gifts for the ordinary world

**When to use it:** Epic fantasy, adventure stories, any narrative driven by a protagonist's transformation.

## Save the Cat (Blake Snyder)

A beat sheet originally designed for screenplays but widely adopted by novelists. Provides specific percentage targets for each beat.

- **Opening Image (0-1%)** -- A snapshot of the protagonist's starting state
- **Theme Stated (5%)** -- Someone hints at the story's thematic question
- **Set-Up (1-10%)** -- Introduce the world, characters, and what's at stake
- **Catalyst (10%)** -- The inciting incident
- **Debate (10-20%)** -- The protagonist wrestles with whether to act
- **Break into Two (20%)** -- The protagonist commits to the journey
- **B Story (22%)** -- A subplot (often a relationship) that carries the theme
- **Fun and Games (20-50%)** -- The promise of the premise delivered
- **Midpoint (50%)** -- Stakes are raised; false victory or false defeat
- **Bad Guys Close In (50-75%)** -- External pressures tighten, internal flaws surface
- **All Is Lost (75%)** -- The lowest point; something or someone is lost
- **Dark Night of the Soul (75-80%)** -- The protagonist processes the loss
- **Break into Three (80%)** -- Inspired by the B-story, the protagonist finds a new way
- **Finale (80-99%)** -- The protagonist storms the castle, armed with new understanding
- **Final Image (99-100%)** -- A snapshot of the protagonist's transformed state

**When to use it:** Plot-driven novels, thrillers, romance, any story needing tight pacing.

## Scene and Sequel Structure

A micro-level framework for building scenes. Every scene should have a clear structure; every sequel (the reaction to a scene) should have its own.

**Scene (Action)**
1. **Goal** -- What the POV character wants right now
2. **Conflict** -- What stands in the way
3. **Disaster** -- The outcome (usually not what the character wanted)

**Sequel (Reaction)**
1. **Reaction** -- The character's emotional response to the disaster
2. **Dilemma** -- The terrible choice the character now faces
3. **Decision** -- The character chooses, setting up the next scene's goal

**When to use it:** Always. This is the sentence-level grammar of narrative structure.
