# Worldbuilding

Worldbuilding is the art of creating a fictional setting that feels as real, complex, and lived-in as the actual world. The goal is not to create an encyclopedia -- it is to create a place where stories feel inevitable because the setting generates its own conflicts, economies, and moral dilemmas.

---

## The Iceberg Principle

Your reader should see 10% of your world. You should know 100%. The unseen 90% gives the visible 10% its weight and texture. When a character mentions something in passing -- a trade route, a religious practice, a historical grievance -- the reader should feel that this detail connects to a larger, coherent system that exists whether or not the story explores it.

---

## Core Systems

Every world (real or fictional) runs on interconnected systems. You don't need to build all of them, but you should know which ones matter to your story.

### Geography and Climate

- Where are the natural resources? Who controls them?
- How does terrain shape culture, trade, and warfare?
- Climate determines agriculture, which determines food, which determines population density, which determines politics.
- Coastlines create trading cultures. Mountains create isolated cultures. Rivers create connected cultures.

### Economics and Trade

- What does your world produce? What does it need?
- Who is rich? Why are they rich? Who is poor? Why are they poor?
- Trade routes create cities. Cities create institutions. Institutions create power structures.
- Currency: what is money? Gold, shells, favors, data, souls? The medium of exchange reveals values.

### Politics and Power

- Who makes decisions? How did they get that power? How do they keep it?
- What are the competing factions? What do they want? What are they willing to do to get it?
- Laws reveal values. Enforcement reveals hypocrisy. The gap between the two is where your story lives.
- Every system has people who benefit and people who are crushed. Both are characters.

### Culture and Religion

- What do people believe? How does belief shape daily behavior?
- Rituals, holidays, taboos, coming-of-age ceremonies, mourning practices -- these are where culture becomes visible.
- Art, music, literature, and architecture are not decoration. They are expressions of values.
- Religion that doesn't affect daily life isn't a religion -- it's set dressing.

### Technology and Magic

- What can people do that they couldn't do a generation ago? Who has access to new capabilities?
- Every technology or magic system has costs, limits, and unintended consequences.
- Magic systems should have rules the reader can understand and internal inconsistencies that create problems.
- The most interesting magic systems have moral costs. What does using the power cost the user?

### History and Lore

- What happened a hundred years ago that everyone remembers? What did everyone forget?
- History is contested. Different cultures remember the same events differently.
- Old conflicts create present tensions. Present tensions create your story.
- Myths and legends are not "fake history" -- they are stories that cultures tell themselves to explain who they are.

## Common Worldbuilding Mistakes

1. **The Theme Park Problem** -- The world exists only as a backdrop for the protagonist's story. There's no sense that anything happened before page 1 or will happen after the last page.
2. **The Encyclopedia Problem** -- The author built a world so detailed they can't resist explaining it all. Info-dumps replace story. The reader signed up for a novel, not a textbook.
3. **The Medieval Europe Default** -- Fantasy especially falls into this trap. Not every world needs to be a thinly reskinned version of 12th-century England with magic and potatoes.
4. **The Homogeneous Culture Problem** -- The entire world shares one religion, one language, one set of values. Real cultures are fractious, diverse, and internally contradictory.
5. **The Static World Problem** -- Nothing changes. Cultures, technologies, and power structures are frozen. Real worlds are always in flux, and change creates conflict.

---

## Worldbuilding for Different Genres

- **Science fiction** -- extrapolate from current science. The worldbuilding is the speculation. Hard SF requires rigorous science; soft SF requires rigorous sociology.
- **Fantasy** -- the magic system is the primary worldbuilding engine. Its rules, costs, and limitations should generate plot naturally.
- **Historical fiction** -- research is worldbuilding. The more accurately you understand the period, the more vividly you can deviate when the story needs it.
- **Magical realism** -- the extraordinary is treated as ordinary. Worldbuilding is subtle, pervasive, and unremarked-upon. The weird is woven into the texture of daily life.
- **Horror** -- the world should feel slightly wrong even before the horror manifests. Something is off about the rules, and the protagonist is the only one who notices.
