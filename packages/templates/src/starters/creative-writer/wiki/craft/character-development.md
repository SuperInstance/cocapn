# Character Development

Characters are the engine of fiction. Plot is what happens; character is why anyone cares. This guide covers the core principles of building characters who feel alive, consistent, and capable of surprising both the reader and themselves.

---

## The Character Triangle

Every compelling character operates on three levels simultaneously:

1. **Want** -- The conscious desire. What the character says they want and actively pursues. This drives the plot forward.
2. **Need** -- The unconscious requirement for growth. What the character actually requires to become whole. This drives the arc.
3. **Wound** -- The past experience or belief that blocks the need. This creates internal conflict and makes the want feel urgent.

The distance between want and need is where dramatic tension lives. A character who gets what they want but not what they need has a tragic arc. A character who gets what they need instead of what they wanted has a transformative arc. A character who gets neither has a bleak arc. All three are valid.

---

## Character Arcs

### Positive Change Arc (Growth)

The character starts with a flawed belief or incomplete understanding. Through the story's events, they confront the flaw, resist it, suffer because of it, and ultimately transform.

1. **Lie the Character Believes** -- The false belief that drives their behavior (e.g., "I have to be perfect to be loved")
2. **The Ghost / Wound** -- The backstory event that installed the lie
3. **The Want** -- The goal that flows from the lie (e.g., "I'll prove I'm perfect by achieving X")
4. **The Need** -- The truth that would heal them (e.g., "I am worthy of love as I am")
5. **The Moment of Truth** -- The scene where the character chooses between the lie and the truth
6. **The Resolution** -- The character acts from the truth, demonstrating lasting change

### Negative Change Arc (Fall)

The character starts with an opportunity for growth but doubles down on their flaw instead. The lie wins. The character ends worse than they started.

Two variants:
- **Corruption arc** -- The character gradually accepts the lie (e.g., Walter White in Breaking Bad)
- **Disillusionment arc** -- The character abandons a truth they once held (e.g., George Orwell's Winston Smith)

### Flat Arc (Steadfast)

The character already holds the truth. The world around them is wrong. By the end, the character's steadfastness changes the world (or the people in it) rather than the character changing themselves.

Examples: Atticus Finch in To Kill a Mockingbird, Sherlock Holmes, Katniss Everdeen (arguably). The character's consistency is the point -- they are the moral anchor around which the story turns.

## Building a Character Bible

A character bible is a living document that tracks everything about your characters. Essential for consistency in long works.

### Core Profile

- Full name, nicknames, aliases
- Age, birthdate, astrological sign (if relevant to the character)
- Physical description -- focus on distinguishing features, not a police report. What would someone notice first?
- Voice and speech patterns -- vocabulary, sentence length, verbal tics, what they talk about vs. what they avoid
- Occupation and relationship to work
- Key relationships -- family, friends, rivals, romantic interests, mentors

### Psychological Profile

- The Lie: the false belief driving their behavior
- The Wound: the backstory event that created the Lie
- The Want: the conscious goal
- The Need: the unconscious requirement for growth
- Fears: specific and visceral, not abstract
- Strengths: what they're genuinely good at (not superpowers -- real competencies)
- Contradictions: the inconsistencies that make them human (a coward who stands up for one specific thing, a generous person who is cruel about one topic)

### Arc Tracking

- Starting state (Act I)
- Key turning points and what triggers them
- Internal resistance points (moments they almost change but pull back)
- Breakthrough moment
- Ending state (Act III)
- Specific scenes where the arc manifests in visible behavior change

## Dialogue as Characterization

Every line of dialogue should do at least one of three things: reveal character, advance plot, or establish setting. Great dialogue does two of three.

- **Vocabulary differentiation** -- Each character should have words they use and words they never use. A marine biologist and a used car salesman describe the same sunset differently.
- **Speech rhythm** -- Sentence length and complexity reveal education, emotional state, and personality. Angry characters use shorter sentences. Analytical characters subordinate.
- **Subtext** -- People rarely say what they mean. The most revealing dialogue is about what characters don't say. Let the reader hear the silence between the words.
- **Self-delusion** -- Characters who say one thing while the reader can see they mean another create instant depth.
