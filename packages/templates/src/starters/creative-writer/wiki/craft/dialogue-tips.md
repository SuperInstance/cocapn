# Dialogue Tips

Dialogue is the most direct window into character. It is also the craft element most writers struggle with, because written dialogue is not real speech -- it is a compressed, shaped, purposeful version of speech that creates the illusion of spontaneity.

---

## The Fundamental Rule

**Written dialogue is not real speech.** Real speech is full of ums, ahs, false starts, repetitions, and meaningless filler. Written dialogue trims all of that while preserving the rhythm and feel of how people actually talk. You are not transcribing; you are composing.

---

## Dialogue Mechanics

### Attribution

Use "said" for most attributions. It is invisible. The reader's eye slides over it. Reserve "whispered," "shouted," "muttered" for moments when the volume actually matters.

Avoid creative attributions like "she hissed," "he ejaculated," "she opined." These draw attention to the attribution instead of the dialogue, which is the wrong direction.

When the speaker is clear from context, drop the attribution entirely. White space between lines of dialogue creates its own rhythm.

### Action Beats

Use action beats (small physical actions) instead of attributions to indicate the speaker and reveal character simultaneously:

```
"I don't think that's a good idea."
Maren set down her pen. "I don't think that's a good idea."
```

The second version tells us who's speaking, shows us her hesitation (setting down the pen), and creates a pause in the rhythm -- all without an attribution.

### Paragraphing

Start a new paragraph for each new speaker. This is non-negotiable. It is how readers track who is talking.

When one character speaks for multiple paragraphs, use opening quotation marks on each paragraph but closing quotation marks only on the final one.

## Subtext and Indirection

**People rarely say what they mean.** The most powerful dialogue operates on two levels: what the character says and what the character means. The gap between these is subtext.

Techniques for creating subtext:

- **Answer a different question.** Character A asks "Do you love him?" Character B says "He's very kind." The evasion is the answer.
- **Change the subject.** Deflection reveals what a character doesn't want to discuss.
- **Physical contradictions.** The body says one thing while the mouth says another. "I'm fine," she said, gripping the table edge.
- **Over-specificity.** A character who gives too much detail is usually lying or anxious. Truth is simple; deception is elaborate.
- **Humor as deflection.** A joke at a tense moment reveals more about a character's emotional state than any serious statement.

---

## Voice Differentiation

Every character should sound distinct enough that the reader can identify the speaker without attribution. This does not mean giving every character a dialect or a catchphrase.

**Techniques for differentiation:**

- **Vocabulary range.** A professor uses longer words and more subordinate clauses than a teenager. A lawyer uses precise language; an artist uses metaphorical language.
- **Sentence length.** Nervous characters speak in short bursts. Confident characters hold the floor. Sad characters trail off.
- **Topic preference.** Each character has subjects they gravitate toward and subjects they avoid. What someone talks about reveals their priorities.
- **Emotional register.** Some characters intellectualize emotions ("That's suboptimal"). Others externalize ("I can't believe this"). Others go physical ("My chest is tight").
- **Information density.** Some characters explain everything. Others say three words and expect you to fill in the rest.

## Common Dialogue Problems

### Talking Heads Syndrome

Characters speak in a featureless white room with no physical grounding. The reader forgets where the characters are.

**Fix:** Anchor dialogue in setting. Characters interact with their environment while talking. They pick things up, look out windows, shift in their seats. The setting isn't a backdrop -- it's a participant in the conversation.

### On-the-Nose Dialogue

Characters say exactly what they mean, feel, and want, all the time. There is no subtext, no indirection, no gap between intention and expression.

**Fix:** Write the on-the-nose version first to understand what the character wants to communicate. Then rewrite it as what the character would actually say. The reader figures out the real meaning from context.

### As-You-Know-Bob Syndrome

Characters tell each other things they both already know, purely for the reader's benefit.

**Fix:** If both characters know the information, find a reason for one of them to bring it up that isn't exposition. Or let the reader figure it out from context. Trust your reader.

### Everyone Sounds the Same

All characters use the same vocabulary, sentence structure, and rhetorical approach.

**Fix:** Write a page of dialogue for each major character with no attributions. Read them aloud. If you can't tell who's talking, the voices aren't differentiated enough.

## Formatting Quick Reference

- Enclose spoken dialogue in double quotation marks (US convention) or single quotation marks (UK convention). Pick one and be consistent.
- Internal thought can be in italics or quotation marks depending on style -- be consistent.
- Use em dashes for interrupted dialogue: "I think we should--" "No."
- Use trailing ellipses for trailing off: "I thought you said..."
- Punctuation goes inside the quotation marks in US convention: "I'm here," she said.
