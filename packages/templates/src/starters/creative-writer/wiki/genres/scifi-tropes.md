# Science Fiction Tropes and Conventions

A guide to the building blocks of science fiction -- the tropes, conventions, and reader expectations that define the genre. Tropes are not cliches. They are shared vocabulary between writer and reader. Knowing them lets you use them deliberately, subvert them intelligently, or replace them with something fresh.

---

## Core Subgenres and Their Promises

Each subgenre makes a different promise to the reader. Breaking that promise without intent frustrates readers. Fulfilling it with craft satisfies them.

### Hard Science Fiction

- **Promise:** Rigorous extrapolation from known science. The science is the story.
- **Reader expectation:** Accurate physics, chemistry, biology, or astronomy. Characters solve problems using science, not magic in lab coats.
- **Key works:** The Martian (Weir), Blindsight (Watts), Seveneves (Stephenson), The Three-Body Problem (Liu).
- **Watch out for:** Info-dumping scientific exposition. Hard SF works best when the science is embedded in action and consequence, not lectured.

### Space Opera

- **Promise:** Sweeping scale, grand conflicts, memorable characters, and a sense of wonder.
- **Reader expectation:** Interstellar civilizations, starship battles, political intrigue across star systems. Science may be soft but the stakes are enormous.
- **Key works:** Dune (Herbert), The Expanse (Corey), Ancillary Justice (Leckie), Culture series (Banks).
- **Watch out for:** Scale without intimacy. The galaxy is at stake, but the reader needs to care about specific people.

### Cyberpunk and Post-Cyberpunk

- **Promise:** High technology, low life. Corporate dystopia. The social consequences of technology.
- **Reader expectation:** Hackers, megacorporations, AI, virtual reality, body modification, urban decay, neon and shadow.
- **Key works:** Neuromancer (Gibson), Snow Crash (Stephenson), Altered Carbon (Morgan), The Windup Girl (Bacigalupi).
- **Watch out for:** The 1980s aesthetic is dated. Contemporary cyberpunk needs to grapple with current technology (social media, surveillance capitalism, gig economy) rather than retro-futurism.

### Climate Fiction (Cli-Fi)

- **Promise:** Near-future speculation about environmental collapse and human response.
- **Reader expectation:** Scientifically grounded scenarios, social and political consequences, human resilience or failure.
- **Key works:** The Ministry for the Future (Robinson), Gold Fame Citrus (Watkins), American War (El Akkad).
- **Watch out for:** Preaching. Cli-fi works best when it shows consequences, not when it lectures about causes.

### Science Fantasy

- **Promise:** The aesthetics and scope of SF with the mythic resonance of fantasy.
- **Reader expectation:** Force fields that function like magic, ancient galactic empires, chosen ones in spaceships, cosmic entities beyond comprehension.
- **Key works:** Star Wars, Dune (arguably), Gideon the Ninth (Muir), The Book of the New Sun (Wolfe).
- **Watch out for:** Incoherent worldbuilding. Even science fantasy needs internal consistency -- the reader should understand the rules, even if those rules are impossible.

## Essential SF Tropes (and How to Use Them)

### The Chosen One / Special Protagonist
- **Standard:** The protagonist is uniquely qualified to save humanity / the galaxy / reality.
- **Subversion:** The protagonist believes they're special but isn't. Or they are special, and it's a burden, not a gift. Or everyone is "special" in this world, and the protagonist is remarkable for being ordinary.
- **Why it persists:** Readers want to identify with the protagonist. Specialness creates investment.

### AI and Artificial Consciousness
- **Standard:** AI becomes self-aware and either threatens humanity (antagonist) or helps it (protagonist/ally).
- **Subversion:** AI that doesn't want to be conscious. AI that is conscious but in ways humans can't recognize. AI that is morally neutral -- not good or evil, just alien.
- **Why it persists:** AI is the mirror we hold up to human consciousness. It lets us ask "what is a person?" from a safe distance.

### The Generation Ship
- **Standard:** A colony ship travels for centuries. The original crew is dead; their descendants have forgotten the mission or reinterpreted it as myth.
- **Subversion:** The crew knows exactly where they're going, and they're lying to the passengers. Or the ship already arrived, but no one remembers.
- **Why it persists:** It compresses all of human history into a bottle. Civilizations rise and fall inside a metal tube.

### First Contact
- **Standard:** Humans meet aliens. Communication is attempted. Conflict or cooperation ensues.
- **Subversion:** The aliens are so genuinely alien that "contact" is barely possible. Or humans are the aliens from another species' perspective. Or first contact already happened, and we didn't notice.
- **Why it persists:** It is the fundamental question: are we alone? And the secondary question: can we understand anything truly other?

### Time Travel and Temporal Paradox
- **Standard:** Characters travel through time, creating or resolving paradoxes.
- **Subversion:** Time travel that doesn't change anything because the past already includes the traveler's intervention. Or time travel that changes everything, constantly, and the protagonist is the only one who notices the timeline shifting.
- **Why it persists:** Regret. Everyone has a moment they wish they could redo.

### The Dystopian Society
- **Standard:** A future society that appears utopian but hides oppression. The protagonist discovers the truth and rebels.
- **Subversion:** The society genuinely is better for most people -- and the protagonist's rebellion threatens genuine progress. Or the dystopia is transparent about its cruelty and the protagonist must decide whether resistance is worth the cost.
- **Why it persists:** It is a vehicle for social critique. Every dystopia is a warning about a present trend.

## Reader Expectations Across the Genre

- **Science matters.** Even soft SF needs a consistent internal logic. The reader agrees to accept your premises; you agree to follow their consequences.
- **Sense of wonder.** SF should evoke awe at the scale and strangeness of the universe. This can be cosmic (galaxies) or intimate (the interior of a cell).
- **Speculation, not prediction.** Good SF is not about guessing the future correctly. It is about asking "what if?" and following the implications to their logical (or illogical) conclusions.
- **The novum.** Every SF story has a novum -- the single new thing (technology, event, discovery) that differentiates the story's world from our own. The entire story should be an exploration of the novum's consequences.
