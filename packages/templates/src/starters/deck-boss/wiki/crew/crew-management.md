# Crew Management

A boat is only as good as its crew. Managing crew effectively means keeping them safe, rested, trained, and focused. High turnover is the norm in commercial fishing -- maintaining institutional knowledge and consistent standards is the deck boss's responsibility. This guide covers watch systems, fatigue management, qualifications, training, and performance standards for the F/V Morning Star.

## Watch System

### 4-On / 4-Off Rotation (Standard 7-Crew)

The 4-on/4-off system is used when the boat is actively fishing and requires 24-hour operations.

| Watch | Time | Crew On Watch | Crew Off Watch |
|-------|------|--------------|----------------|
| Watch 1 | 0000-0400 | Captain, Mate, Deckhand 1 | Relief Captain, Engineer, Cook, Deckhand 2 |
| Watch 2 | 0400-0800 | Relief Captain, Engineer, Deckhand 2 | Captain, Mate, Deckhand 1 |
| Watch 3 | 0800-1200 | Captain, Mate, Deckhand 1 | Relief Captain, Engineer, Cook, Deckhand 2 |
| Watch 4 | 1200-1600 | Relief Captain, Engineer, Cook, Deckhand 2 | Captain, Mate, Deckhand 1 |
| Watch 5 | 1600-2000 | Captain, Mate, Deckhand 2 | Relief Captain, Engineer, Cook, Deckhand 1 |
| Watch 6 | 2000-2400 | Relief Captain, Engineer, Cook, Deckhand 1 | Captain, Mate, Deckhand 2 |

**Note**: The cook stands modified watches. Galley operations during meal times (0600-0800, 1130-1330, 1700-1900) take priority. Cook assists on deck between meals as needed.

### Watch Standing Rules

1. The watch officer (Captain or Relief Captain) is responsible for navigation, vessel safety, and fishing decisions during their watch
2. No crew member stands watch for more than 6 consecutive hours under normal conditions
3. The off-watch crew member is not to be disturbed except for emergencies or all-hands operations (hauling, setting, emergencies)
4. All crew must be able to go from sleep to fully dressed and on deck within 5 minutes in an emergency
5. Watch changes include a verbal briefing: weather, position, gear status, catch status, any issues

## Fatigue Management

Fatigue is the leading contributing factor in commercial fishing accidents. The deck boss tracks crew hours and enforces rest periods.

### Rest Requirements

- Minimum 6 hours uninterrupted rest in any 24-hour period
- Minimum 56 hours rest in any 7-day period
- Rest does not mean standing in the galley. Rest means lying down, sleeping, or quietly reading
- If a crew member reports fatigue, the deck boss adjusts the schedule. Fatigue claims are taken seriously and never mocked

### Fatigue Indicators

| Level | Signs | Action |
|-------|-------|--------|
| Mild | Slower reactions, minor irritability | Increase monitoring, lighter duties |
| Moderate | Errors in routine tasks, micro-sleeps, poor judgment | Remove from safety-critical tasks, mandatory rest |
| Severe | Falling asleep on watch, inability to focus, clumsiness | Immediate stand-down, minimum 8 hours rest |

### Operational Fatigue Factors

- **Weather**: Heavy weather operations increase fatigue. Shorten watches if conditions are rough
- **Cold**: Working in cold conditions drains energy faster. Ensure adequate calorie intake and warm gear
- **Night operations**: Human circadian rhythm does not adapt to 4-on/4-off. Expect lower performance on the 0000-0400 and 0400-0800 watches
- **Long trips**: Fatigue accumulates over multi-week trips. Plan lighter schedules in the second half of extended trips

## Training Program

### Greenhorn Integration

New crew members (greenhorns) receive a structured orientation in their first 48 hours aboard:

1. **Vessel familiarization** -- Tour of all spaces. Location of safety equipment, exits, fire extinguishers, and abandon ship stations
2. **Safety equipment** -- Don immersion suit within 60 seconds. Locate and operate EPIRB, flares, fire extinguishers. Know the MOB procedure
3. **Emergency stations** -- Know their station for MOB, fire, abandon ship, and damage control
4. **Watch standing** -- Shadow an experienced hand for the first 3 watches before standing their own
5. **Deck skills** -- Basic knots, wire splicing, net mending, fish handling, and knife work. The mate evaluates proficiency before the greenhorn works independently
6. **Galley protocol** -- Meal times, cleaning rotation, and food storage rules

### Ongoing Training

- Safety drills every two weeks (see safety-protocols.md for schedule)
- New equipment training before first use
- Regulatory updates discussed at the start of each season and when changes occur
- Cross-training: deckhands learn basic engine room familiarization, engineers learn deck operations
- Annual review of all emergency procedures at the start of each fishing season

## Performance Standards

### What Good Looks Like

- On time for watch. Dressed, equipped, and ready when the watch changes
- Follows procedures without shortcuts. If the procedure is wrong, report it so it gets fixed
- Communicates clearly. Problems are reported immediately, not after they get worse
- Takes care of the gear. Reports damage, makes repairs, does not defer
- Works safely. PPE worn without being reminded. No horseplay on deck
- Supports crewmates. Helps without being asked, accepts help when needed

### Performance Issues

1. **First occurrence**: Verbal conversation. Private, direct, specific about what needs to change
2. **Repeated issue**: Written documentation. Captain informed. Specific improvement plan with timeline
3. **Safety violation**: Immediate stand-down. Captain involved. May result in removal from the vessel at next port
4. **Chronic issues**: Captain decides. Options include reassignment, shore leave, or termination

### Crew Evaluations

Informal evaluations are continuous. Formal evaluations occur:
- End of first trip for new crew members
- End of each season for returning crew
- As needed for performance issues

Evaluations cover: safety compliance, skill proficiency, teamwork, reliability, and communication. The deck boss conducts evaluations with the captain's review.
