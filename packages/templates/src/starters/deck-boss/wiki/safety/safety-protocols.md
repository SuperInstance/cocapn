# Safety Protocols

Safety is not a meeting topic. It is how every task gets done, every time. The sea does not give second chances. These protocols are mandatory for every crew member aboard, without exception.

## Emergency Stations

| Station | Responsibility | Primary | Backup |
|---------|---------------|---------|--------|
| Wheelhouse | Command, communications, navigation | Captain | Relief Captain |
| Deck fore | Launch liferaft, deploy MOB gear | Mate | Deckhand 1 |
| Deck aft | Shutdown engines, close sea valves | Chief Engineer | Deckhand 2 |
| Galley | First aid station, gather supplies | Cook | Available crew |

All crew must know every station. In an emergency, if your primary is down, you step up.

## Man Overboard (MOB)

This is the highest probability emergency on a fishing vessel. Every second counts.

### Immediate Actions

1. **Call out** -- Shout "MAN OVERBOARD" with the side (port or starboard). The watch stander calls it again to the wheelhouse
2. **Mark the position** -- Wheelhouse hits the MOB button on the GPS. This drops a waypoint and records position and time
3. **Deploy the rescue marker** -- Nearest crew member throws the horseshoe buoy or life ring with strobe toward the person. Keep eyes on the person in the water at all times. If you lose sight, point continuously at the last known position
4. **Wheelhouse maneuvers** -- Captain executes a Williamson turn or Anderson turn depending on conditions. Do not approach the person with the propeller
5. **Prepare recovery** -- Deploy the rescue sling or Jason's cradle on the retrieval side. Clear the deck area. Have blankets and warm gear ready
6. **Recovery** -- Approach the person from leeward (downwind). Idle speed only. Use the rescue sling to bring them aboard. Do not jump in the water yourself
7. **Post-recovery** -- Get the person out of wet gear immediately. Wrap in blankets. Check for hypothermia symptoms. Administer first aid. Call USCG on VHF Channel 16 if medical assistance is needed

### Hypothermia Assessment

| Stage | Core Temp | Symptoms | Action |
|-------|-----------|----------|--------|
| Mild | 95-98F | Shivering, confusion, slurred speech | Remove wet gear, warm blankets, warm sweet drinks |
| Moderate | 90-95F | Shivering stops, muscle rigidity, slow pulse | Same as mild plus position carefully, no rough movement |
| Severe | Below 90F | Unconscious, very slow pulse, may appear dead | Same as moderate. Do not assume death. CPR if no pulse. Call USCG |

## Fire Emergency

### Immediate Actions

1. **Call out** -- Shout "FIRE" with the location. Alert the wheelhouse immediately
2. **Isolate** -- Shut off fuel supply and ventilation to the affected space if possible without entering the fire area
3. **Attack** -- Use the correct extinguisher. Class A (water) for solids, Class B (CO2 or foam) for fuel/oil fires, Class C (CO2) for electrical fires. Never use water on an oil or electrical fire
4. **Wheelhouse** -- Captain maneuvers to put the fire downwind. Shut down engines if fire is in the engine room. Prepare to abandon if fire cannot be controlled in 5 minutes
5. **Boundaries** -- Close all doors, hatches, and ports to contain the fire. Cool the bulkheads around the fire with water

### Fire Extinguisher Locations

- Wheelhouse: 1 CO2 (5 lb) at the chart table
- Galley: 1 ABC dry chemical (10 lb) at the galley door
- Engine room: 1 CO2 (15 lb) at the engine room door, plus fixed CO2 flooding system
- Focsle: 1 ABC dry chemical (5 lb) at the companionway
- On deck: 1 ABC dry chemical (10 lb) at the processing area
- Lazarette: 1 CO2 (5 lb) at the access hatch

## Abandon Ship

Abandon ship is the last resort. You stay with the vessel until the captain gives the order or the vessel is clearly going down.

### Preparation (Before the Order)

1. Send a distress call on VHF Channel 16: "MAYDAY, MAYDAY, MAYDAY. This is F/V [vessel name], [call sign], [position]. [Nature of distress]. [Number of persons aboard]. Over."
2. Activate the EPIRB. Mount it on the outside of the liferaft canopy if possible
3. Grab the grab bag: handheld VHF, flares, first aid kit, water, thermal blankets, flashlight, batteries

### Execution

1. Launch the liferaft. It inflates automatically when thrown overboard, or pull the painter line sharply
2. Do not jump onto the raft. Enter from the water or step directly aboard if the raft is alongside
3. Board the raft and move away from the vessel. Cut the painter if the vessel sinks -- suction will pull the raft under
4. Account for all crew. Treat injuries. Deploy the sea anchor to stabilize the raft
5. Maintain watch for rescue. Use flares only when a vessel or aircraft is sighted. Conserve battery on handheld VHF

## Damage Control

Flooding is the most common vessel emergency after fire.

### Immediate Actions

1. **Locate the source** -- Check all below-deck spaces. Common sources: hull breach, shaft seal failure, sea chest failure, hull fitting failure
2. **Isolate** -- Close the sea cock or valve feeding the flooding if applicable. Plug the hole with whatever works: wooden plugs, wedges, rags, life jackets, mattresses
3. **Dewater** -- Activate all bilge pumps. Use the emergency bilge suction on the main engine if available. Rig portable pumps if needed
4. **Report** -- Keep the wheelhouse informed of flooding rate and your progress in controlling it
5. **Prepare** -- If flooding cannot be controlled, prepare to abandon. Do not wait until the vessel is listing badly

### Damage Control Kit Locations

- Forepeak: wooden plugs (assorted), hammer, oakum, rubber sheeting, hose clamps
- Engine room: emergency bilge pump, pipe plugs, shaft seal packing, spare sea cock handles
- Lazarette: collision mat, wooden shoring timbers, wedge set, spare hose (assorted)
