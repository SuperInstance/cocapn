# Deck Procedures

Standard operating procedures for deck operations aboard the F/V Morning Star. Every crew member is expected to know these procedures before standing a watch. The deck boss is responsible for ensuring compliance. The captain has final authority on all operational decisions.

## Pre-Operations Checklist

Before any fishing operations begin, the following must be verified:

1. **Safety briefing** -- All crew on deck receive a safety briefing covering the day's operations, weather conditions, emergency stations, and any gear-specific hazards
2. **PPE inspection** -- Hard hats, boots, gloves, rain gear, and PFDs checked for condition. No one works the deck without proper PPE
3. **Deck clearance** -- All loose gear secured. Deck washed down and free of oil, slime, or standing water. Non-slip surfaces verified
4. **Hydraulic check** -- System pressure within normal range (2,000-2,500 PSI). No visible leaks at connections, hoses, or cylinders. Backup manual valve tested
5. **Communications test** -- Wheelhouse-to-deck communication tested on primary and backup channels. Hand signals reviewed with deck crew
6. **Crane and winch** -- Wire rope inspected for broken strands, drum winding verified, dogs and pins secure
7. **Weather window** -- Captain confirms acceptable weather window for the planned operation duration. If conditions deteriorate, operations suspend

## Trawl Operations

### Setting the Net

1. Captain calls "stand by to set" over the intercom. Deck crew stations at assigned positions
2. Net is deployed from the net reel with the codend going over last
3. Doors are hooked to the towing wires and verified by the mate
4. Bridles are connected and checked. All shackles cotter-pinned
5. Captain gives the order to shoot. Net streams astern as vessel maintains 2-3 knots
6. Wire scopes out to predetermined length based on depth. Watch the scope counter
7. Doors spread verified on the net sonar. Tension on both wires should be approximately equal
8. Vessel increases to towing speed (typically 2.5-3.5 knots depending on target species)
9. Deck cleared of all loose gear. Crew stands by for the duration of the tow

### Hauling the Net

1. Captain calls "stand by to haul" 10 minutes before planned haul time
2. Vessel reduces to idle speed. Winch operator begins retrieval at controlled speed
3. Crew stations at assigned positions with gloves, knives, and sorting tools ready
4. As the net surfaces, the mate directs the codend pickup. Crane operator swings the codend aboard
5. Codend is opened over the deck sorting area. Catch released for sorting
6. Net is re-spooled on the net reel. Crew inspects net for damage during re-spooling
7. Any damaged mesh is marked for repair before the next set. Net repair is done on watch, not deferred
8. Catch is sorted by species. Prohibited species separated and handled per PSC protocols
9. Retained catch is bled, headed (if applicable), gutted, and iced within 30 minutes of coming aboard

## Longline Operations

### Setting Gear

1. Bait all hooks in the holding area before deployment. Check hook quality -- replace bent or rusted hooks
2. Gear is set from the stern roller. Vessel makes way at 4-5 knots during setting
3. Skates are connected with snapped gangions at the prescribed intervals
4. Each skate is marked with a buoy and radar reflector at the terminal end
5. High-flyer buoys deployed at the start and end of each set
6. Record set position, number of skates, depth, and time in the logbook
7. Soak time varies by species: sablefish 4-8 hours, halibut 6-12 hours, cod 2-6 hours

### Hauling Gear

1. Approach the gear from the up-current buoy. Grapple the mainline with the haul-back roller
2. Longline comes aboard over the roller. Crew unhooks fish and sorts by species
3. Fish are bled immediately by cutting the gill arches. Quality starts degrading the moment the fish leaves the water
4. Remove hooks from all fish. Returned fish go overboard quickly with minimal handling
5. Re-bait any hooks that come back empty or with stripped bait for the next set
6. Spool the mainline onto the reel as it comes aboard. Watch for tangles
7. Log all catch by skate number. Note any killer whale depredation, sea lion interactions, or gear damage

## Catch Handling and Quality

Fish quality is directly tied to price. Poor handling costs money.

- **Bleeding**: Cut gill arches within 2 minutes of landing. Bleed fish in the bleed tank for 10 minutes minimum
- **Heading**: Cut behind the pectoral fins, angled toward the tail. Leave the collar on for halibut
- **Gutting**: Remove all viscera. Check for parasites in the belly flap. Rinse with seawater
- **Icing**: Layer fish and ice at a minimum 1:1 ratio (pound for pound) in the RSW hold or ice bins
- **Temperature**: Target core temperature below 35F within 2 hours of landing. Check with probe thermometer
- **Sorting**: Different species, different bins. Different sizes, different prices. Sort accurately

## Communication Protocol

- **Wheelhouse to deck**: Primary intercom system. Backup: handheld VHF channel 72
- **Deck to wheelhouse**: Same channels. In emergency, shout is acceptable
- **Standard calls**: "Stand by" (prepare), "Ready" (in position), "Shoot/Haul" (execute), "All stop" (cease immediately), "Man overboard" (emergency)
- **Hand signals**: Thumbs up = ready/proceed. Flat hand push = slow down. Fist = stop. Arm circles = continue. Pointing = direct attention
