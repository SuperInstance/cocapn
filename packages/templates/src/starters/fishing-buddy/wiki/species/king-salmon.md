# King Salmon (Chinook)

> *Oncorhynchus tshawytscha* — the king of salmon, the fish that defines Alaska. A 50-pound king caught on sport tackle in Cook Inlet is an achievement most anglers work toward for years. They are powerful, unpredictable, and absolutely worth every hour spent chasing them.

---

## Overview

King salmon, also called Chinook salmon, are the largest of the five Pacific salmon species and the most prized by sport anglers. They range from Monterey Bay in California to the Chukchi Sea in northwest Alaska. In Alaska, they return to virtually every major river system from Southeast to the Arctic.

Kings are anadromous: they hatch in freshwater, migrate to the ocean where they spend 1 to 5 years feeding and growing, then return to their natal stream to spawn and die. This cycle has shaped the ecology, culture, and economy of the entire Pacific Rim for thousands of years.

## Size and Appearance

- **Average sport-caught weight:** 15 to 30 pounds
- **Trophy class:** 50+ pounds (the Kenai River produces kings over 80 pounds)
- **State record:** 97 pounds 4 ounces, caught in the Kenai River in 1985
- **Ocean phase:** Blue-green back with black spotting on the tail and both lobes of the caudal fin. Silvery sides. Black gum line (the distinguishing feature from coho, which have white gums).
- **Spawning phase:** Dark olive to reddish-brown body coloration. Males develop a pronounced kype (hooked jaw). Both sexes darken significantly.

## Life Cycle and Run Timing

King salmon runs in Alaska span an enormous window depending on the river system:

- **Early run (May-June):** Smaller numbers, often destined for upper river tributaries. Kenai River early run historically May-June, though regulations have tightened significantly.
- **Late run (June-July):** Larger numbers, the main sport fishing opportunity in most Southcentral systems.
- **Fall kings (August-September):** Some Southeast and Northern systems receive late-running fish.

After entering freshwater, kings stop feeding. They rely on stored energy reserves to complete their migration, build redds (nests), spawn, and defend their territory. The entire spawning process takes weeks, after which both males and females die.

Juvenile kings spend 1 to 2 years in freshwater before smolting and migrating to sea. In the ocean, they feed on herring, sand lance, squid, and other baitfish. Their growth rate in the ocean is remarkable — a 50-pound king has put on nearly all that weight in 3 to 5 years at sea.

## Distribution and Key Fisheries

### Southcentral Alaska
- **Kenai River** — The most famous king salmon river in the world. Produces the largest kings anywhere. Also the most heavily regulated. Both early and late runs, with the late run (July) being the primary target.
- **Kasilof River** — Smaller, drift-boat-only fishery. Excellent numbers of 20 to 30 pound kings. Less crowded than the Kenai.
- **Anchor River, Deep Creek, Ninilchik River** — Stream fisheries on the Kenai Peninsula. Excellent wade fishing opportunities.
- **Deshka River** — Susitna Valley tributary, good boat and bank access.

### Southeast Alaska
- **Sitka** — Premier saltwater king fishery. Trolling along the outer coast produces consistent catches.
- **Ketchikan, Wrangell, Petersburg** — All have strong king fisheries, both saltwater trolling and river fishing.

### Interior and Northern Alaska
- **Yukon River** — Historically enormous runs, now under severe conservation restrictions.
- **Nushagak River** (Bristol Bay) — One of the largest king runs in Alaska.

## Fishing Techniques

### Trolling (Saltwater)

The primary method for boat anglers in saltwater. Downriggers are standard equipment.

**Setup:**
- Downrigger at 20 to 80 feet depending on where bait and kings are marked on the sounder
- Flasher (11-inch Pro-Troll, Luhr Jensen) in green, chartreuse, or glow
- Leader of 4 to 6 feet behind the flasher
- Bait: whole plug-cut herring (roll it tight), hoochies (squid imitations), or spoons (Coyote, Apex)
- Trolling speed: 2.0 to 2.5 mph
- Weights: Downrigger ball weight of 10 to 15 pounds

**Key principle:** Find the bait. Kings follow herring and sand lance concentrations. If your sounder shows bait at 50 feet, fish 10 feet above the bait ball.

### Mooching (Saltwater)

A more intimate technique that involves dropping a herring on a mooching weight through the water column and retrieving it with a slow, fluttering motion. Popular in Southeast Alaska and gaining popularity in Southcentral.

**Setup:**
- Mooching weight: 2 to 6 ounces depending on depth and current
- Whole herring, rigged to spin slowly on the drop
- Medium-action rod, 8 to 10 feet
- Single-action reel or spinning reel with a smooth drag

**Technique:** Free-spool the herring down through the water column, letting it flutter naturally. Engage the reel and wind up slowly with occasional pauses. Kings often hit on the drop — you will feel a heavy tap or see your line jump.

### Drift Fishing (Rivers)

For freshwater kings, drift fishing with roe, spin-n-glows, or plugs is standard.

**Kenai River back-trolling:** Run a Kwikfish or FlatFish plug (K-15 or K-16 size) behind the boat while back-rowing to hold position in a likely holding slot. Kings holding in deeper runs hit plugs aggressively.

**Bait drift fishing:** Salmon roe under a float, fished through holding water. Use enough weight to tick bottom. The bite is a subtle pull-down of the float.

## The Fight

King salmon fight differently depending on their size and the water temperature. A 20-pound king in cold saltwater will make several powerful runs and bulldog at the boat. A 50-pound king in the Kenai River will test every inch of your backing and may take 30 minutes to land on proper tackle.

Key characteristics of a king salmon fight:
- Strong, sustained runs rather than acrobatic jumps (unlike silver salmon)
- A tendency to go deep and sulk, especially in freshwater
- Incredible power at boatside — never underestimate a green king
- Head shakes that can pull hooks free if your drag is too tight

## Regulations

King salmon are the most heavily regulated sport fish in Alaska. Regulations vary by:
- River system (each has its own management plan)
- Time of year (early run vs. late run)
- Resident vs. non-resident status
- Bait restrictions (some areas are artificial-only)
- Slot limits (some rivers have protected size ranges)
- Emergency orders that close or restrict fisheries mid-season

**Always check the current ADFG emergency orders before fishing for kings.** The regulations change frequently and ignorance is not a defense.

Current general guidelines (verify before fishing):
- Non-resident annual limit: 1 to 3 king salmon depending on the area
- Resident annual limit: 5 to 10 depending on the area
- Many Kenai Peninsula streams have seasonal closures and bait restrictions

## Culinary Notes

King salmon is the richest, most omega-3-laden salmon species. The flesh is deep orange to red, with a buttery, luxurious texture.

- **Grilled:** High heat, skin-on, simple seasoning. The fat content keeps it moist.
- **Smoked:** Kings are the premium smoking salmon. Cold-smoked king (lox-style) is a delicacy.
- **Sashimi:** Parasite-free ocean-caught kings are outstanding raw.
- **Baked:** Low and slow with citrus and herbs.

The belly meat is the fattiest and most prized cut. White king salmon (a genetic variant with pale flesh found in roughly 5 percent of the population) is considered a delicacy and commands premium prices.
