# Pacific Halibut

> *Hippoglossus stenolepis* — the flatfish that built Alaska's sport fishing reputation. From 10-pound "chickens" to 400-pound barn doors, halibut are the reason thousands of anglers trailer boats to Homer every summer.

---

## Overview

Pacific halibut are the largest of all flatfish and among the largest fish in the Pacific Ocean. They are right-eyed flounders, meaning both eyes sit on the upper (right-facing) side of their laterally compressed body. Their upper side is mottled olive-brown to near-black, blending perfectly with the ocean floor. Their blind side is white.

Halibut begin life swimming upright like normal fish. During their first year, the left eye migrates over the top of the skull to the right side, and the fish settles onto the ocean floor on its left side for the rest of its life. This metamorphosis is one of the more remarkable transformations in the animal kingdom.

## Size and Growth

- **Average sport-caught weight:** 15 to 40 pounds (these are the "chicken halibut" prized for eating)
- **Trophy class:** 100+ pounds (the barn doors)
- **Maximum recorded:** Over 500 pounds; the IFGA record is 459 pounds, caught near Dutch Harbor
- **Growth rate:** Slow. A 100-pound halibut is approximately 12 to 15 years old. Females grow larger and live longer than males.
- **Age at maturity:** Females around 12 years; males around 8 years

## Distribution in Alaska

Halibut are found throughout Alaska's coastal waters from the Southeast panhandle to the Aleutian Islands and into the Bering Sea. Key sport fishing areas include:

- **Cook Inlet** (Homer, Anchor Point, Ninilchik) — the halibut capital of the world
- **Prince William Sound** (Valdez, Cordova, Whittier)
- **Southeast Alaska** (Ketchikan, Sitka, Juneau)
- **Kodiak Island** — excellent numbers and size
- **Resurrection Bay** (Seward) — good fishing closer to shore

## Habitat and Behavior

Halibut spend most of their lives on or near the ocean floor at depths ranging from 20 feet to over 1,000 feet. In sport fishing contexts, most are caught between 60 and 400 feet. They prefer sandy, gravelly, or muddy bottoms adjacent to structure — underwater ledges, pinnacles, and drop-offs are prime territory.

Halibut are ambush predators. They lie partially buried in the sediment, waiting for prey to pass overhead. Their diet includes herring, sand lance, capelin, pollock, cod, octopus, crab, and even other flatfish. They are opportunistic feeders and will move into shallower water to feed during tide changes, especially on incoming tides.

Seasonal movements follow a general pattern: deeper water in winter for spawning (November through March at depths of 600 to 1,500 feet), then a migration to shallower summer feeding grounds. The spawning cycle is tightly linked to the lunar phase and water temperature.

## Fishing Techniques

### Bottom Fishing (the primary method)

Anchor or drift over known halibut grounds. Drop bait to the bottom and wait. The key is keeping your bait stationary or moving very slowly along the bottom.

**Rod:** Medium-heavy to heavy action, 5 to 7 feet. Stand-up rods rated for 30 to 80 pound line.
**Reel:** Conventional reel with a strong drag system. Penn Squall, Avet, Shimano Triton.
**Line:** 65 to 80 pound braid mainline with a 10 to 15 foot monofilament or fluorocarbon leader (80 to 150 pound test).
**Weight:** 16 to 32 ounces depending on depth and current. Cannonball sinkers or lead on a dropper loop.
**Hook:** 12/0 to 16/0 circle hooks. Mandatory in many areas for halibut conservation.

### Bait

The gold standards:
- **Salmon heads** — strip the cheek and jaw sections. Halibut love them and the scent trail is enormous.
- **Herring** — whole, plug-cut, or strip. Fresh or frozen, herring are reliable.
- **Octopus** — tough, stays on the hook, scent carries well. Often paired with herring.
- **Squid** — readily available, works as a supplement to other baits.
- **Artificials** — large white or chartreuse grubs, Berkley Gulp, and butterfly jigs all produce, especially when fish are aggressive.

### Jigging

Vertical jigging with 8 to 16 ounce butterfly jigs or leadhead jigs tipped with bait is increasingly popular. Work the jig with sharp lifts of the rod tip followed by a controlled fall. Halibut often hit on the drop. Effective in shallower water (under 200 feet) or when fish are actively feeding.

## Identifying a Bite

Halibut bites range from subtle to violent. Small halibut often tap-tap-tap the bait. Large halibut may simply load up the rod like you hooked the bottom. The classic approach: when you feel the bite, let the fish take line for a few seconds, then reel down until you feel weight, and lift into the fish. Circle hooks set themselves when you maintain steady pressure — do not swing to set the hook.

## Handling and Safety

Halibut have incredibly powerful bodies and can injure anglers in the boat. A green (un-tired) halibut over 50 pounds can destroy a cockpit. Best practices:

1. Tire the fish thoroughly before bringing it alongside
2. Use a harpoon or gaff for fish over 50 pounds — never lip-grip a large halibut
3. Subdue the fish with a club or priest before bringing it aboard
4. Bleed the fish immediately by cutting the gill arches — this dramatically improves meat quality
5. Store on ice, never let the flesh sit in water

**Never** bring a large, active halibut into the boat. The thrashing can break rods, rip out electronics, and cause serious injury.

## Culinary Notes

Halibut is one of the finest eating fish in the world. The flesh is firm, white, mild-flavored, and versatile. Fillet yield averages 50 to 60 percent of round weight.

- **Grilled:** Brush with olive oil, season simply, grill over medium-high heat. Halibut steaks hold together beautifully.
- **Beer-battered:** The classic Alaska shore lunch. Cold beer in the batter for maximum crunch.
- **Baked:** Parmesan-crusted halibut is a crowd favorite.
- **Ceviche:** The firm texture holds up perfectly to acid curing.
- **Smoked:** Cold-smoked halibut is a delicacy, though less common than smoked salmon.

The cheeks are considered the finest cut — tender, sweet, and often reserved for the captain.

## Conservation

Pacific halibut are managed by the International Pacific Halibut Commission (IPHC), a joint US-Canada body established in 1923. Sport fishing regulations in Alaska include daily bag limits, annual limits for non-residents, size restrictions, and mandatory circle hooks in many areas. Always check current ADFG emergency orders before fishing, as limits can change mid-season.

Catch-and-release mortality for halibut caught in deep water is a concern. Fish caught from over 200 feet may suffer barotrauma. If releasing, use a descending device or weighted release to return the fish to depth quickly. Handling time should be minimized.
