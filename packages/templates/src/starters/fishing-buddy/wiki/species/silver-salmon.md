# Silver Salmon (Coho)

> *Oncorhynchus kisutch* — the acrobat. Pound for pound the hardest-fighting salmon in Alaska, silvers hit like a freight train and jump like a largemouth on Red Bull. August in Alaska means silver season, and the fish know it too.

---

## Overview

Silver salmon, also called coho salmon, are the sport angler's favorite salmon. They are aggressive, acrobatic, and numerous. They hit lures and flies with reckless abandon, leap repeatedly when hooked, and are outstanding eating. The silver salmon run defines late summer fishing across Alaska, from the dipnet fisheries of the Kenai to the fly-in streams of Bristol Bay.

Silvers are the third most abundant Pacific salmon species (behind pinks and sockeye) and return to virtually every coastal stream and river system in Alaska. Their willingness to take a wide variety of offerings makes them accessible to every skill level, from first-time spin casters to seasoned fly anglers.

## Size and Appearance

- **Average sport-caught weight:** 8 to 14 pounds
- **Trophy class:** 20+ pounds (fish over 20 pounds are exceptional)
- **State record:** 26 pounds 2 ounces
- **Ocean phase:** Bright silver sides, dark metallic blue-green back. Black spots on the back and the upper lobe of the tail only (distinguishes them from kings, which are spotted on both tail lobes). White gum line (distinguishes them from kings, which have black gums).
- **Spawning phase:** Males develop a deep red body coloration with dark backs and a pronounced kype. Females darken to a more subdued maroon. Both sexes develop darker heads.

## Life Cycle and Run Timing

Silver salmon have a relatively simple life cycle compared to other Pacific salmon:

1. **Freshwater phase:** Fry emerge from the gravel in spring and spend 1 to 2 years rearing in freshwater streams, sloughs, and lakes.
2. **Smolt migration:** Out-migrate to the ocean in spring as smolts.
3. **Ocean phase:** Spend approximately 18 months at sea feeding voraciously on herring, sand lance, capelin, and squid. Growth is explosive during this period.
4. **Return:** Migrate back to their natal stream from July through October depending on the system.

Run timing by region:
- **Southeast Alaska:** July through September, peaking in August
- **Prince William Sound:** July through September, peaking in mid-August
- **Cook Inlet / Kenai Peninsula:** Late July through September, peaking mid-August to early September
- **Bristol Bay:** July through August
- **Kodiak:** July through September

Many river systems have distinct early and late runs of silvers, extending the fishing season by several weeks.

## Distribution and Key Fisheries

### Saltwater Fisheries
- **Homer Spit / Kachemak Bay** — Silvers congregate at river mouths and can be caught trolling or mooching from boats. The Nick Dudiak Fishing Lagoon on Homer Spit is a shore-based silver hotspot.
- **Resurrection Bay (Seward)** — Excellent silver fishing from boat and shore. The Seward Silver Salmon Derby in August is the oldest fishing derby in Alaska.
- **Sitka, Ketchikan** — Southeast saltwater trolling for silvers is world-class.

### Freshwater Fisheries
- **Kenai River** — Late-run silvers in September and October. Fish to 20+ pounds. Drift boats and power boats.
- **Kasilof River** — Strong silver run in August. Drift boats only.
- **Anchor River, Deep Creek** — Wade-accessible stream fishing on the Kenai Peninsula.
- **Russian River** — Fly-fishing only in sections. Excellent silver fishing in September.
- **Bristol Bay tributaries** — Fly-in float trips with spectacular silver fishing in remote settings.

## Fishing Techniques

### Spin Fishing

Silvers are aggressive and will hit a wide variety of hardware:

**Spoons:** Pixie spoons (pink, orange, chartreuse), Krocodile spoons, and Vibrax spinners in sizes 3 through 5. Cast and retrieve with a slow-to-medium pace. The flutter on the drop triggers strikes.

**Spinners:** Vibrax, Blue Fox, and Mepps in sizes 3 to 5. Silver, blue, and pink blades with fluorescent bodies. In murky water, go larger and brighter. In clear water, scale down.

**Plugs:** Small diving plugs (Wee Warts, Wiggle Warts) in silver, gold, and pink patterns. Trolled or cast and retrieved in holding water.

**Bait:** Salmon roe under a float or drifted along the bottom. Single eggs in clear water, egg clusters in stained water. Silvers will also hit sand shrimp and prawn.

### Fly Fishing

Silver salmon are one of the premier fly-rod targets in Alaska. The takes are visual and violent.

**Flies:** Clouser Minnows, Deceivers, and baitfish patterns in pink, white, and chartreuse. Streamer patterns like the Dolly Llama and Starlight Leech. Surface flies like pollywog patterns elicit explosive topwater strikes in calm water.

**Rigging:** 7 to 9 weight fly rod with a floating or intermediate sink-tip line. Leader of 3 to 4 feet of 12 to 16 pound fluorocarbon. In deep holding water, a fast-sink shooting head gets the fly down quickly.

**Retrieve:** Strip with moderate speed. Vary the retrieve until you find what triggers strikes. Silvers often follow the fly for several feet before committing, so keep stripping until the fly is at your feet.

### Trolling (Saltwater)

Effective from boats in bays and along shorelines near river mouths.

**Setup:**
- Downrigger at 20 to 50 feet, or troll shallow with a diving planer
- Flasher in green or chartreuse
- Hoochie (squid imitation) in pink, orange, or glow
- Herring strip behind a dummy flasher
- Trolling speed: 2.5 to 3.0 mph (slightly faster than king trolling)
- Lure length behind the flasher: 24 to 36 inches for hoochies

## The Fight

Silver salmon are the most acrobatic of the Pacific salmon. When hooked, they typically:

1. Make a searing initial run that empties your reel of 50 to 100 yards of line
2. Leap repeatedly, sometimes 5 to 6 feet clear of the water, head-shaking violently
3. Change direction suddenly and unpredictably
4. Continue fighting hard even as they approach the boat or bank

The key to landing silvers is managing the jumps. Keep your rod tip low when they are in the air (to prevent them from throwing the hook by snapping the line) and maintain steady pressure. Do not try to horse them — they will break light line or straighten hooks.

## Catch and Release Considerations

Silvers that are still ocean-bright (chrome) handle catch and release reasonably well if properly released. Fish that have begun to color up for spawning have lower survival rates. Best practices for catch and release:

- Use barbless hooks or pinch down the barbs
- Minimize air exposure — keep the fish in the water as much as possible
- Use wet hands or a rubber net (never a cloth net)
- Revive the fish facing upstream in current before release
- If a fish is bleeding from the gills, it should be kept if legal to do so

## Culinary Notes

Silver salmon are excellent eating, though leaner than king salmon. The flesh is orange-red, moderately firm, and has a clean salmon flavor.

- **Grilled:** Skin-on fillets over high heat. Silvers cook quickly — pull them when they are just done.
- **Pan-seared:** Cast-iron skillet, skin-side down, medium-high heat. Crispy skin and moist flesh.
- **Smoked:** Hot-smoked silver is outstanding. The lower fat content compared to kings produces a firmer smoked product.
- **Canned:** Silvers are the traditional canning salmon in Alaska. Pressure-canned in pint jars, they keep for years and are a pantry staple.
- **Fish tacos:** Grilled or blackened silver chunks in corn tortillas with cabbage slaw and chipotle crema.

Chrome-bright ocean silvers are the finest eating. As they color up in freshwater, the flesh quality degrades. Eat them silver, not red.

## Conservation

Silver salmon populations are generally healthy across Alaska, though specific systems may experience weaker returns in any given year. The ADFG monitors returns in real-time and may issue emergency orders restricting harvest on specific runs. As with all Alaska fisheries, check current regulations before fishing.
