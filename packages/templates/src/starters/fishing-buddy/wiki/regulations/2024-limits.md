# 2024 Alaska Sport Fishing Regulations Summary

> **IMPORTANT: This is a reference guide, not an official regulation document.** Always verify current limits with the Alaska Department of Fish and Game (ADFG) before fishing. Emergency orders (EOs) can change limits and seasons at any time during the season. When in doubt, call ADFG or check their website.

---

## General Rules

### Licensing

- **Resident annual sport fishing license:** Required for all Alaska residents age 18 and older.
- **Non-resident annual sport fishing license:** Required for all non-residents age 16 and older.
- **Non-resident short-term licenses:** Available in 1-day, 3-day, 7-day, and 14-day durations.
- **King salmon stamp:** Required for anyone fishing for king salmon (resident and non-resident), in addition to the base license. Must be purchased separately.
- **Military:** Active duty military stationed in Alaska may purchase a resident license.
- **Youth:** Alaska residents under 18 and non-residents under 16 fish for free but must comply with all bag and possession limits.

Licenses are available online at the ADFG website, at most sporting goods stores, and at many grocery stores in Alaska.

### Definitions

- **Daily bag limit:** The maximum number of fish you may take in a calendar day.
- **Possession limit:** The maximum number of fish you may have in your possession at any time. Typically equals the daily bag limit, but in some cases it is the daily limit times the number of days fished (up to a cap).
- **Annual limit:** A cap on the total number of a species you may harvest in a calendar year. Primarily applies to king salmon and halibut for non-residents.
- **Slot limit:** A protected size range. Fish within the slot must be released immediately. Common for Kenai River king salmon.
- **Emergency Order (EO):** An in-season regulation change issued by ADFG when run strength, escapement, or other factors require immediate action. EOs can open, close, or restrict fisheries on short notice.

### General Prohibitions

- **Snagging:** Illegal in most Alaska sport fisheries. Fish must be hooked in the mouth (inside the jaw). However, fish hooked elsewhere must be kept if legal size and species (they count toward your limit).
- **Chumming:** Prohibited in freshwater. Allowed in saltwater in some areas.
- **Live bait:** Prohibited in all Alaska freshwater. Herring and other bait may be used in saltwater.
- **Barbed hooks:** Required to be barbless in some specific waters. Check area-specific regulations.
- **Retention of alive fish:** Generally prohibited. Fish retained must be killed immediately.

## Southcentral Alaska Saltwater Regulations (2024)

### Pacific Halibut

Managed by the International Pacific Halibut Commission (IPHC) with additional state regulations:

- **Daily bag limit:** 2 halibut per person per day
- **Size restriction:** One fish of any size, one fish 32 inches or under (total length). This "under 32" rule ensures the second fish is a smaller "chicken" halibut.
- **Annual limit (non-resident):** The IPHC sets annual limits that can change yearly. For 2024, non-residents are subject to an annual limit. Check the current IPHC and NOAA Fisheries regulations.
- **Season:** Typically February 1 through December 31, but subject to change.
- **Circle hooks:** Required when fishing for halibut with bait in Alaska saltwater.
- **Guided vs. unguided:** Different rules may apply to guided (charter) anglers vs. unguided (private boat) anglers. Charter operators must follow additional restrictions including logbook requirements and size reporting.

### King Salmon (Saltwater)

- **Daily bag limit:** 1 per day (general Southcentral saltwater limit)
- **Annual limit (non-resident):** Typically 1 to 3 per year depending on the specific area. Some areas require recording the date and location of each king harvest on the back of your license.
- **King salmon stamp:** Required in addition to your fishing license.
- **Size minimum:** 28 inches in most saltwater areas (fish under 28 inches are considered "jack" kings and may not be retained in some areas).

### Silver Salmon (Saltwater)

- **Daily bag limit:** 3 per day (general Southcentral saltwater)
- **Annual limit:** No annual limit for silvers
- **Possession limit:** 6 (2 daily limits)

### Pink Salmon (Saltwater)

- **Daily bag limit:** 6 per day
- **Annual limit:** No annual limit

### Sockeye Salmon (Saltwater)

- **Daily bag limit:** 3 per day (some areas may be 6 depending on run strength)
- **Annual limit:** No annual limit

### Rockfish

- **Daily bag limit (combined species):** 4 per day in most Southcentral waters
- **Yelloweye rockfish:** Additional restrictions may apply, including a sub-limit within the aggregate bag limit. Some areas require use of a descending device for released rockfish.
- **Non-pelagic rockfish closure:** Some areas have seasonal closures for non-pelagic species to protect vulnerable populations.
- **Conservation concern:** Rockfish are extremely long-lived and slow-reproducing. Yelloweye can live over 100 years. Catch-and-release mortality is high for deep-water species. Target pelagic species when possible.

### Lingcod

- **Season:** Typically July 1 through December 31 in Southcentral Alaska. Closed January through June to protect spawning fish.
- **Daily bag limit:** 1 per day in most areas
- **Size minimum:** 35 inches in most Southcentral waters
- **Possession limit:** 1 (same as daily limit)

### Pacific Cod and Other Bottomfish

- **Daily bag limit (combined):** Generally liberal — check area-specific regulations
- **No annual limits**

## Kenai River Regulations (2024)

The Kenai River has the most complex regulations in Alaska. Below is a summary; always verify with ADFG.

### King Salmon — Kenai River

**Early Run (January 1 through June 30):**
- Subject to frequent emergency order closures. In recent years, the early run has been heavily restricted or closed entirely to protect weak returns.
- Bait is typically prohibited during the early run.
- Check current EO status before planning an early-run trip.

**Late Run (July 1 through July 31):**
- Daily bag and possession limit: 1 king salmon per day
- Annual limit for non-residents: varies by EO, typically 1 to 2 total from the Kenai
- Size restrictions: Slot limits may apply (protecting large spawning fish)
- Bait is allowed from July 1 (unless restricted by EO)
- Gear restrictions: Single hook only in some sections
- Guided angler restrictions: Different rules may apply to guided vs. unguided

**Upper Kenai:** Closed to king salmon fishing year-round.

### Sockeye Salmon — Kenai River

- **Daily bag limit:** 3 to 6 per day depending on run strength and EO status
- **Season:** Open in July and August, specific dates set annually
- **Technique restrictions:** Some areas are fly-fishing-only (Russian River confluence area)
- **Russian River:** Separate management plan with different limits. Typically 3 per day with a Russian River-specific endorsement on your license.

### Silver Salmon — Kenai River

- **Daily bag limit:** 2 per day (general river limit)
- **Season:** July through October, specific dates vary by section
- **Bait:** Allowed in most sections, check area-specific rules

### Rainbow Trout and Dolly Varden — Kenai River

- **Catch and release only** for rainbow trout in the Upper Kenai (above Skilak Lake outlet)
- **Season:** Some sections closed April through June to protect spawning trout
- **Bait restrictions:** Artificial lures only in many sections to reduce catch-and-release mortality
- **No retention** of rainbow trout over 16 inches in most sections

## Homer and Kachemak Bay Regulations (2024)

### Saltwater Species

- **Halibut:** Same as general Southcentral saltwater rules (2 per day, one under 32 inches)
- **King salmon:** 1 per day, king stamp required
- **Silver salmon:** 3 per day
- **Rockfish:** 4 per day combined species
- **Lingcod:** 1 per day, 35 inch minimum, July 1 through December 31

### Nick Dudiak Fishing Lagoon (Homer Spit)

- Special regulations apply to this stocked fishery
- Snagging is prohibited
- King salmon and silver salmon seasons are announced annually by ADFG
- Separate from the general Cook Inlet regulations in some respects

## Dipnet Fisheries

Alaska residents may participate in personal-use dipnet fisheries, which are separate from sport fishing:

- **Kenai River dipnet:** Open to Alaska residents only. Typically July. Requires a separate dipnet permit.
- **Kasilof River dipnet:** Open to Alaska residents. Different dates and limits.
- **Fish wheel permits:** Available in some areas for personal use.
- **Limits:** Typically 25 salmon per head of household plus 10 per additional household member (exact limits vary by fishery and year).

Dipnet permits, harvest reporting, and dates are managed separately from sport fishing licenses.

## Emergency Order (EO) Awareness

Emergency orders are the single most important regulatory tool during the fishing season. ADFG biologists monitor returns in real-time and can:

- **Close** a fishery entirely (no retention, catch and release only, or complete closure)
- **Reduce** bag limits from the published regulation
- **Increase** bag limits when returns are stronger than expected
- **Restrict** gear types (e.g., artificial lures only, single hook only)
- **Modify** seasons (open early, close early, extend seasons)

**How to check current EOs:**
1. ADFG website: the Emergency Orders page for each management area
2. ADFG offices: Call the local office (Soldotna for Kenai River, Homer for Kachemak Bay)
3. Local tackle shops: Most post current EOs and are happy to share information
4. Social media: ADFG posts EO announcements on their social media channels
5. In-person: Rangers and wildlife troopers patrol fishing areas and can inform you of current restrictions

**Violations of emergency orders carry the same penalties as violating published regulations.** Ignorance of a current EO is not a defense.
