# Tackle Box Guide

> The essential gear for Alaska saltwater and freshwater fishing, organized by species and technique. Whether you are building a tackle box from scratch or filling gaps in an existing setup, this guide covers what you actually need — not what the catalog wants to sell you.

---

## The Core Saltwater Tackle Box

This is the tackle you need on the boat for a general Alaska saltwater trip targeting halibut, salmon, and rockfish out of Homer, Seward, or anywhere on the Kenai Peninsula.

### Halibut Gear

**Hooks:**
- 16/0 and 14/0 circle hooks (Owner, Gamakatsu, Eagle Claw). Buy them in bulk (25+ packs) because you will lose them to the bottom.
- Snell your own hooks on 100 to 130 pound monofilament leader material for the best action and strength. Pre-snelled hooks are convenient but the loop-to-loop connection can be a weak point.

**Weights:**
- 16 oz, 24 oz, and 32 oz cannonball sinkers. Start with two of each per angler.
- A slider snap swivel to attach the weight to the main line (allows the weight to slide freely).
- Spare weight is essential — you will lose sinkers to the bottom.

**Leader Material:**
- 100 to 150 yard spool of 80 to 130 pound monofilament leader material (Ande, Momoi, Yo-Zuri).
- 100 to 150 yard spool of 80 to 100 pound fluorocarbon leader (more abrasion-resistant but more expensive).
- Build leaders 10 to 15 feet long. The leader absorbs the shock of head shakes and bottom contact.

**Swivels and Hardware:**
- 200 pound barrel swivels (Sampo, Rosco) for connecting main line to leader.
- 250 pound snap swivels for attaching weights.
- Spreader bars (optional) for separating the weight from the leader.

**Bait Equipment:**
- Bait-cutting board (a simple plastic cutting board works)
- Sharp fillet knife for cutting salmon heads and herring
- Bait thread or magic thread for securing cut bait to hooks
- Rubber bands for securing salmon head strips

### Salmon Trolling Gear

**Downrigger Equipment:**
- Downrigger release clips (Offshore OR16 or Scotty pinch pads). Carry spares — they break and get lost.
- Downrigger weights: 10 to 15 pound cannonballs. Finned or torpedo styles.
- Terminal snaps and swivels for connecting release clips.

**Flashers:**
- 11-inch flashers in green/chartreuse, glow white, and silver/blue (Pro-Troll, Luhr Jensen, Silver Horde). Three minimum per color.
- 8-inch dodgers for a more subtle presentation.
- Spare snaps and swivels for flasher connections.

**Hoochies (Squid Imitations):**
- Glow white/chartreuse (the workhorse color)
- Pink/white (for silvers)
- Army truck green/brown (for kings)
- Blue splatterback (clear water)
- Buy 10 to 20 hoochies minimum — they are inexpensive and you will lose some.

**Herring Rigs:**
- Tandem hook rigs for whole herring (size 3/0 and 5/0 hooks)
- Crown hooks and helmet rigs for spinning herring
- Herring filet knife or bait knife

**Spoons:**
- Coyote 3.5 and 4.0 spoons in cop car, green/silver, and blue/silver
- Apex spoons in hot pink and chartreuse
- Luhr Jensen Krocodile spoons in nickel and chrome/blue

**Leader Material for Salmon:**
- 30 to 40 pound fluorocarbon leader material for hoochies and herring (36 to 48 inch leaders)
- 40 to 50 pound monofilament for spoon leaders (60 inch leaders behind flashers)

### Rockfish and Lingcod Gear

**Jigs:**
- 4 to 8 oz leadhead jigs with 6 to 8 inch curly-tail grubs (white, chartreuse, motor oil)
- 6 to 12 oz butterfly jigs (Duel Hardcore, Shimano Butterfly) in pink, blue mackerel, and zebra glow
- 8 to 14 oz knife jigs (Point Wilson Dart, crippled herring) for deep water

**Swimbaits:**
- 6 to 8 inch paddle-tail swimbaits (Berkley, Savage Gear, Castaic) in sardine and herring patterns
- Rig on 1 to 4 oz weighted jig heads

**Dropper Loop Rigs:**
- Pre-tied or hand-tied with 60 to 80 pound leader and 4/0 to 6/0 hooks
- Two-hook rig with 8 to 16 oz weight on the bottom

## The Freshwater Tackle Box (Kenai Peninsula Rivers)

### Salmon Gear (Sockeye, Silver, Pink)

**Spinners:**
- Vibrax size 3 through 5 in silver, blue, and fluorescent pink
- Mepps Aglia size 3 through 5 in silver and fire tiger
- Blue Fox Vibrax in silver, brass, and chartreuse

**Spoons:**
- Pixie spoons in pink, orange, and chartreuse (the Homer Spit standard)
- Krocodile spoons in nickel, brass, and blue/silver

**Salmon Eggs:**
- Jarred salmon roe (Berkley, Pautzke) in red and orange
- Mesh spawn bags in various colors
- Egg loops for tying roe to hooks

**Hooks and Weights:**
- Size 2 through 2/0 hooks for egg fishing (Gamakatsu Octopus in red or pink)
- Pencil lead and rubbercore sinkers (1/4 to 1 oz)
- Corkies and Cheaters in various colors for buoyancy

**Flies (for Sockeye Flossing):**
- Coho flies in red, orange, and pink (size 2 through 6 hooks)
- Comets and shrimp patterns
- Simple yarn flies in fluorescent colors

### Trout and Dolly Varden Gear

**Beads:**
- 6mm to 10mm hard beads in salmon egg colors: Glo-Roe (bright orange), dark red, peach, and milky pink
- Toothpicks or pegs for securing beads 1 to 2 inches above the hook

**Flies:**
- Flesh flies (white, peach, and natural) in size 4 through 8
- Sculpin patterns (olive and natural) in size 4 through 6
- Egg-sucking leech in purple and black (size 4 through 6)
- Beadhead Prince nymph, size 8 through 12
- Large streamers (Dolly Llama patterns) in smolt and sculpin colors

**Float Fishing Rig:**
- 8 to 11 foot float rod (moderate action)
- Center pin reel or spinning reel with smooth drag
- 8 to 12 pound main line
- 6 to 10 pound fluorocarbon leader
- Floats (Drennan, Raven) in various sizes for adjusting to depth and current

## Rod and Reel Combinations

### Halibut / Bottom Fish Setup
- **Rod:** 5 to 7 foot medium-heavy to heavy action conventional rod (Shimano Trevala, Daiwa Saltiga)
- **Reel:** Conventional reel with at least 20 pounds of drag (Penn Squall 30, Avet MXJ, Shimano Talica)
- **Line:** 65 to 80 pound braid mainline
- **Cost:** $200 to $500 for a quality setup

### Salmon Trolling Setup
- **Rod:** 8 to 9 foot medium action downrigger rod with a sensitive tip (Daiwa Heartland, Shimano Talora)
- **Reel:** Levelwind conventional reel with line counter (Daiwa Sealine, Shimano Tekota)
- **Line:** 25 to 30 pound monofilament or 40 to 50 pound braid
- **Cost:** $150 to $350 for a quality setup

### Salmon Spin Fishing (Freshwater)
- **Rod:** 8 to 9 foot medium action spinning rod (St. Croix Premier, Shimano Convergence)
- **Reel:** Spinning reel size 3000 to 4000 (Shimano Stradic, Daiwa BG)
- **Line:** 15 to 20 pound braid with 12 to 15 pound fluorocarbon leader
- **Cost:** $100 to $250 for a quality setup

### Fly Fishing (Trout and Salmon)
- **Rod:** 7 to 9 weight, 9 foot fly rod (Orvis Clearwater, Echo Boost)
- **Reel:** Large arbor fly reel with a strong, smooth drag (Ross Reels, Lamson)
- **Line:** Weight-forward floating line (trout) or sink-tip line (salmon)
- **Cost:** $200 to $500 for a quality setup

## Essential Boat Equipment

Beyond tackle, every Alaska fishing boat should carry:

**Safety (required):**
- PFD (life jacket) for every person on board — wear it, do not just stow it
- Throwable flotation device (Type IV)
- Fire extinguisher (rated for marine use)
- Sound-producing device (horn or whistle)
- Navigation lights (for low visibility and night)
- VHF radio — monitor Channel 16 for emergencies
- First aid kit with pressure bandages

**Fishing Essentials:**
- Landing net (large enough for halibut and salmon)
- Gaff (for halibut over 50 pounds)
- Harpoon and dart (for large halibut)
- Fish club or priest (for dispatching fish)
- Bleeding bucket or cut-off milk jug for bleeding fish
- Fillet knife with a stiff blade and sharpener
- Cooler with ice (500 quart minimum for a full day of halibut fishing)
- Fish bags (insulated) for transporting fish

**Navigation and Electronics:**
- GPS/Chartplotter with Alaska charts
- Depth sounder/fish finder (color, with bottom hardness discrimination)
- Compass (backup to electronics)
- Marine charts for the area you are fishing

**Anchoring:**
- Anchor appropriate for your boat size (Danforth or Bruce pattern)
- Anchor rode: 300 to 600 feet of rope (5:1 scope minimum)
- Anchor buoy and trip line for easy retrieval

## Maintenance Tips

1. **Rinse everything in freshwater** after every saltwater trip. Rods, reels, tackle, and boat hardware all corrode if not rinsed.
2. **Reel maintenance:** Remove side plates and rinse the internals. Re-lubricate with marine grease. Service drags before each season.
3. **Line replacement:** Replace braided line every 2 to 3 seasons. Replace monofilament leaders after every trip. UV exposure and salt degrade line faster than most anglers realize.
4. **Hook sharpening:** Sharpen hooks before each trip. A sticky-sharp hook catches more fish and penetrates faster, reducing deep-hooking.
5. **Tackle storage:** Use waterproof tackle boxes with rust inhibitors. Silica gel packets in tackle boxes absorb moisture and prevent corrosion.
