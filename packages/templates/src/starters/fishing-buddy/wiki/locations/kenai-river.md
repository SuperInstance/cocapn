# Kenai River

> The most famous salmon river in Alaska — and arguably the world. The Kenai produces the largest king salmon on the planet, hosts the chaotic spectacle of the sockeye run, and delivers outstanding silver salmon fishing into October. It is 82 miles of glacier-fed water that has shaped the lives and livelihoods of everyone who lives along its banks.

---

## Overview

The Kenai River drains the Kenai Peninsula in Southcentral Alaska, flowing 82 miles from Kenai Lake near Cooper Landing westward to Cook Inlet at the city of Kenai. It is a glacial river — turquoise-green from suspended glacial flour — and it runs through some of the most accessible and developed fishing country in Alaska.

The Kenai is divided into two management sections:

**Upper Kenai River** — From Kenai Lake downstream to the Sterling Highway bridge at Soldotna (Skilak Lake outlet to the bridge is the most commonly referenced "Upper Kenai"). This section is drift-boat friendly, has fly-fishing-only water near the Russian River confluence, and is renowned for sockeye and silver salmon fishing.

**Lower Kenai River** — From the Sterling Highway bridge downstream to the mouth at Cook Inlet. This is power boat water, wider, deeper, and the primary arena for king salmon fishing. The lower river is where the 97-pound state record king was caught.

## River Characteristics

- **Length:** 82 miles
- **Source:** Kenai Lake (glacial)
- **Mouth:** Cook Inlet at Kenai, Alaska
- **Water type:** Glacial — visibility typically 2 to 4 feet
- **Major tributaries:** Russian River, Moose River, Killey River, Skilak Lake (which divides the upper and lower river)
- **Peak flow:** July (driven by glacial melt)
- **Winter flow:** Low and clear, with significant anchor ice in cold years

## Species and Seasons

### King Salmon (Chinook)

The Kenai's crown jewel. Two distinct runs:

**Early Run (May-June):** Smaller numbers of fish, but includes some of the largest kings of the year. These fish are destined for the upper river and tributaries like the Killey River. Early run kings have been under significant conservation restrictions in recent years, with frequent closures.

**Late Run (June-July):** The main event. Large numbers of big kings returning to lower and middle river spawning grounds. The late run peaks in early July and produces the river's famous 50 to 80 pound fish. This is the most competitive and crowded fishery on the river.

**Techniques:** Back-trolling Kwikfish and FlatFish plugs is the standard method on the lower river. Some anglers drift fish with roe or spin-n-glows. The upper river is closed to king fishing.

**Regulations:** Extremely restrictive and subject to emergency orders. Non-resident annual limit is typically 1 to 2 kings from the Kenai. Bait restrictions, slot limits, and complete closures are common. Always verify current ADFG regulations.

### Sockeye Salmon (Red)

The Kenai's most chaotic fishery. The sockeye run brings thousands of anglers to the river's banks, standing shoulder to shoulder, flossing reds in a spectacle that has to be seen to be believed.

**Run timing:** Late June through August, with the peak in mid-July to early August.

**Technique:** "Flossing" — a technique where a long leader (3 to 6 feet) with a coho fly or small yarn fly is drifted through holding fish. The line slides through the open mouth of a salmon and the hook catches in the corner of the jaw. It is controversial but legal and is the dominant method.

**Location:** The banks from Soldotna downstream to the Moose River confluence and below are the combat fishing zones. The Russian River confluence (Upper Kenai) is another major sockeye hotspot with its own management plan.

**Bag limit:** Typically 3 to 6 per day depending on run strength, with emergency order adjustments.

### Silver Salmon (Coho)

Excellent silver fishing from August through October. The late fall silvers on the Kenai are large, bright, and aggressive.

**Run timing:** August through October. Early run in August, late run in September-October.

**Technique:** Spin fishing with Vibrax spinners, drifting roe under a float, or fly fishing with streamers. Silvers hold in the deeper runs and slots throughout the lower and upper river.

**Advantage:** Far less crowded than the king or sockeye fisheries. September and October silvers offer a more peaceful Kenai River experience with excellent fishing.

### Rainbow Trout and Dolly Varden

The Kenai is a world-class rainbow trout river. Catch-and-release for wild rainbows is the standard, and the fish grow enormous on the salmon egg and flesh diet provided by the spawning salmon runs.

**Season:** June through October. The best fishing is in September and October when salmon are spawning and the trout and dollies are gorging on eggs and decaying salmon flesh.

**Techniques:** Bead fishing under a float — using single beads in salmon egg colors (orange, red, peach) to imitate loose eggs drifting downstream. Also productive: flesh flies, sculpin patterns, and large streamers.

**Fly fishing only:** A section of the Upper Kenai near the Russian River confluence is designated fly-fishing-only water.

### Pink Salmon (Humpy)

Odd-numbered years only (2023, 2025, etc.) bring massive pink salmon runs to the Kenai. Pinks are aggressive and easy to catch on light tackle, making them a great species for kids and beginners.

## Access Points

**Lower River (Power Boat):**
- **City of Kenai docks** — Launch near the mouth. Access to the lower river and Cook Inlet.
- **Eagle Rock ramp** — Mid-river access, popular for king salmon fishing.
- **Soldotna Creek Park** — Near the Sterling Highway bridge. Bank fishing and boat launch.
- **Izaak Walton State Recreation Site** — River access and camping.

**Upper River (Drift Boat):**
- **Jim's Landing** (at Skilak Lake outlet) — Popular drift boat launch.
- **Sterling Highway bridge (Soldotna)** — Takeout for upper river drifts.
- **Russian River confluence** — Bank access via Russian River Campground (U.S. Forest Service). Combat fishing for sockeye.
- **Ferry Bridge** (Cooper Landing area) — Upper access near Kenai Lake.

**Bank Access:** Numerous roadside pullouts and public access points along the Sterling Highway between Soldotna and Cooper Landing provide walk-in access to the upper river.

## Boat Considerations

**Lower River:** Power boats are standard. The lower river is wide and deep enough for most vessels, but shallow gravel bars shift seasonally. A propeller guard is highly recommended. Jet boats are ideal for navigating the shifting channels.

**Upper River:** Drift boats and rafts only (no motors allowed in most sections). Several commercial drift boat guides operate on the upper river. The float from Jim's Landing to the bridge takes 6 to 8 hours with fishing stops.

**Skilak Lake:** This large, glacial lake can be dangerous in wind. Small boats should cross it with caution. Most anglers launch below the lake rather than navigating through it.

## Local Knowledge

**"The Pillars"** — A series of rock formations on the lower river where kings hold. A local landmark for navigation.

**"Eagle Rock"** — A major king salmon holding area on the lower river. Also the name of a nearby boat ramp.

**The "Moose River confluence"** — Where the Moose River meets the Kenai, creating a wide, shallow area that concentrates sockeye and silvers. Popular bank fishing spot.

**"Honeymoon Cove"** — A back-channel on the upper river that holds big rainbows in the fall.

**June Rise:** The annual surge in water level from glacial melt that typically occurs in mid-June. It changes the river's character significantly — higher, faster, dirtier water.

## Tips for Fishing the Kenai

1. **Hire a guide for your first king salmon trip.** The Kenai is a complex river with subtle holding water, strong current, and heavy boat traffic. A guide will put you on fish and teach you the techniques.
2. **Bring chest waders for sockeye.** You will be standing on slippery rocks in fast current. Felt-soled or studded wading boots are essential.
3. **Respect the combat fishing zones.** The sockeye fishery is crowded. Be patient, give your neighbors space, and follow the unwritten etiquette: fish in a line, no cross-casting, and help each other net fish.
4. **Check emergency orders daily during king season.** The ADFG can close the king fishery on 24 hours' notice.
5. **September silvers are the secret.** Everyone leaves after the sockeye run. The silver fishing in September and October is world-class with a fraction of the crowd.

## Nearby Amenities

The towns of Soldotna and Kenai provide all services: hotels, campgrounds, tackle shops, grocery stores, boat rentals, fuel, and restaurants. The Sterling Highway connects the Kenai River corridor to Anchorage (about 3 hours by road) and to Homer (about 2 hours south). The Kenai Municipal Airport has daily flights to Anchorage.
