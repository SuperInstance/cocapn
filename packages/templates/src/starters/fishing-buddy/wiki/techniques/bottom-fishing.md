# Bottom Fishing

> The bread and butter of Alaska saltwater fishing. Drop it down, hold on, and reel it up. Simple in concept, nuanced in execution. Bottom fishing produces halibut, rockfish, lingcod, and Pacific cod — the backbone species of any Alaska saltwater trip.

---

## Overview

Bottom fishing is the technique of presenting bait or lures on or near the ocean floor. In Alaska, this primarily targets halibut, rockfish, lingcod, and Pacific cod. It is the most productive technique for putting fish in the box, and it requires less specialized gear than trolling or mooching.

Bottom fishing can be done while anchored, drifting, or at a controlled hover. The choice depends on the target species, the current, the depth, and the structure being fished. Each approach has distinct advantages.

## Anchored Bottom Fishing

Anchoring over a productive piece of structure is the most controlled approach. It allows you to present baits precisely and keep them in the strike zone indefinitely.

**When to anchor:**
- Fishing specific ledges, pinnacles, or drop-offs where halibut concentrate
- Moderate current that allows baits to sit relatively still
- Situations where you want to chum or create a scent trail
- Fishing with multiple anglers who need to stay organized

**Technique:**
1. Motor up-current of your target area and drop the anchor, calculating the setback distance so the boat settles directly over the structure.
2. Set the anchor firmly and let the boat settle on the rode.
3. Drop baits to the bottom and maintain contact with a taut line.
4. When you feel a bite, let the fish take a short amount of line, then reel down and lift into the fish.
5. Re-anchor if the tide or wind swings the boat off the structure.

Anchoring in deep water (over 200 feet) or strong current requires significant scope — typically 5:1 or more rope-to-depth ratio. A buoy system on the anchor allows quick release and retrieval.

## Drift Fishing

Drifting over structure covers more water and is the preferred method when fish are scattered or when anchored fishing is impractical due to current or depth.

**When to drift:**
- Fish are spread over a large area rather than concentrated on a specific piece
- Strong current makes anchoring difficult or dangerous
- Searching for new productive bottom
- Targeting rockfish or lingcod along rock walls and ledges

**Technique:**
1. Position the boat up-current or up-wind of the area you intend to fish.
2. Deploy baits to the bottom before the boat drifts over the target zone.
3. Maintain bottom contact by feeding line as the boat moves. You should feel the weight tapping bottom every few seconds.
4. When a fish hits, reel immediately to set the hook and get the fish's head coming up. This is critical to prevent the fish from wrapping your line in structure.
5. If the drift speed is too fast, use a drift sock or sea anchor to slow the boat.

**Drift speed** matters enormously. Ideal drift speed for bottom fishing is 0.5 to 1.5 knots. Faster than 2 knots and you will not maintain bottom contact without excessive weight.

## Tackle Setup

### The Standard Halibut Rig

The go-to rig for Alaska bottom fishing is simple and effective:

**Main line:** 65 to 80 pound braided line (Daiwa J-Braid, PowerPro). Braid has zero stretch, which provides excellent sensitivity at depth and instant hooksets.

**Leader:** 10 to 15 feet of 80 to 150 pound monofilament or fluorocarbon. The leader absorbs shock during the fight and provides abrasion resistance near the bottom.

**Weight:** 16 to 32 ounce cannonball sinker on a slider or snap swivel. Go heavier in deep water or strong current. In 300 feet with a running tide, you may need 48 ounces.

**Hook:** 12/0 to 16/0 circle hook on a snell knot or tied directly to the leader. Circle hooks are mandatory in many Alaska saltwater fisheries and dramatically reduce deep-hooking mortality.

**Spreader bar (optional):** A wire spreader bar separates the weight from the leader, preventing tangles. Common on charter boats but not essential for private boaters.

### Rockfish and Lingcod Gear

For rockfish and lingcod, the setup shifts toward artificial lures:

**Jigs:** 4 to 16 ounce leadhead jigs with large curly-tail grubs (6 to 10 inches) in white, chartreuse, or motor oil. Butterfly jigs (Duel, Shimano) in pink, blue-silver, or zebra glow are highly effective.

**Swimbaits:** 6 to 8 inch paddle-tail swimbaits in sardine, herring, or perch patterns rigged on weighted jig heads.

**Metal jigs:** 6 to 14 ounce vertical jigs worked with sharp lifts and controlled falls. Lingcod and yelloweye rockfish slam these on the drop.

**Dropper loop rigs:** A weight on the bottom with one or two hooks on short dropper loops above. Effective for rockfish when they are stacked in the water column.

## Bait Selection

### Halibut Bait

- **Salmon heads** (stripped into strips): The gold standard. Enormous scent trail, stays on the hook well, and halibut absolutely crush it.
- **Herring** (whole or plug-cut): Available everywhere, catches all species. Change it frequently as it gets washed out.
- **Octopus strips:** Tough, durable, excellent scent. Often combined with herring on the same hook.
- **Squid**: Good backup bait, especially for smaller halibut.
- **Cod strips**: When nothing else is available, strips of Pacific cod work. Not preferred but functional.

### Rockfish and Lingcod Bait

- **Herring** on a dropper loop rig
- **Cod strips**
- **Whole herring** for larger lingcod
- **Artificials outfish bait** for these species in most situations

## Reading the Bottom

A quality depth sounder (fish finder) is essential for productive bottom fishing. Key features to identify:

**Ledges and drop-offs:** Halibut stack up on the edges of underwater ledges where the bottom drops from one depth to another. Fish the transition zone where the slope changes.

**Pinnacles and seamounts:** Underwater rock piles and pinnacles attract rockfish, lingcod, and halibut. Work all sides of the structure.

**Flat sandy bottom:** Don't ignore open sand and mud. Halibut spread out over flat bottom to feed, especially during tide changes. The "hog line" — where a flat bottom transitions to a slope — is prime water.

**Hard bottom vs. soft bottom:** Rock and gravel hold more life than featureless mud. Your sounder can distinguish bottom hardness by the thickness and intensity of the bottom return.

**Bait balls:** Clouds of baitfish on the sounder mean predators are nearby. Halibut and rockfish position themselves below and adjacent to bait concentrations.

## Tide and Timing

Tides profoundly affect bottom fishing success:

**Tide change (slack):** The period of minimal current when the tide switches direction. This is prime time for halibut. Bait stays in the strike zone without excessive weight, and fish feed actively during the reduced current.

**Incoming tide:** Generally more productive than outgoing. Baitfish are pushed inshore, and predatory fish follow. The last two hours of the incoming tide and the first hour of slack are the golden window.

**Strong running tides:** Difficult to fish effectively. Heavy current requires more weight, baits do not sit still, and boat control becomes challenging. Some anglers plan their fishing entirely around tide slacks for this reason.

**Depth adjustments:** As the tide changes, the depth of productive structure changes relative to the surface. A ledge that is at 180 feet at low tide might be at 165 feet at high tide. Adjust your presentation accordingly.

## Safety Considerations

Bottom fishing in Alaska saltwater carries specific risks:

**Lead weight hazards:** A 2-pound lead sinker swinging around the deck is a weapon. Keep the deck organized and never cast or swing heavy weights.

**Deep water releases:** Fish caught from deep water (over 100 feet) may suffer barotrauma. Use a descending device for rockfish releases. Halibut caught deep should be dispatched or released quickly.

**Weather changes:** Alaska marine weather changes fast. Monitor VHF weather broadcasts and have a float plan. A calm morning can turn into a small craft advisory by afternoon.

**Line management:** Braided line under tension can cut skin and fingers to the bone. Never wrap line around your hand or fingers. Use a gaff, harpoon, or net for landing large fish.

**Boat positioning:** When anchored in current, always approach the anchor rode from down-current. Never stand in a bight of anchor line.
