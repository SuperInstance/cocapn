# Trolling

> Cover water, find fish, catch salmon. Trolling is the most consistent method for putting salmon in the boat in Alaska saltwater. It is part science, part art, and entirely addictive when the rod tips start bouncing.

---

## Overview

Trolling is the technique of dragging lures or bait behind a moving boat to entice strikes from salmon and other pelagic species. In Alaska, trolling is the primary method for targeting king salmon and silver salmon in saltwater. It is also effective for lake trout in freshwater and can be adapted for pink salmon, sockeye (in the ocean phase), and even halibut in shallow water.

The beauty of trolling is that it covers water efficiently. Instead of waiting for fish to come to you, you go find them. A properly trolled spread of gear searching at multiple depths can locate actively feeding salmon quickly. Once you find the depth and the pattern that is working, you can duplicate it and fill the box.

## Downrigger Trolling

Downriggers are the standard tool for controlled-depth trolling in Alaska. A downrigger uses a heavy weight (typically 10 to 15 pounds) on a steel cable to carry your fishing line to a precise depth. A release clip attached to the weight holds your line and pops free when a fish strikes, transferring the fish directly to your rod.

### Downrigger Setup

**Downrigger:** Manual or electric. Scotty and Cannon are the most common brands in Alaska. Electric downriggers are worth the investment if you fish deep (over 100 feet) frequently.

**Weight:** 10 to 15 pound cannonball. Heavier weights track better in strong current and at higher speeds. Avoid torpedoes with fins — they can cause erratic tracking.

**Release clip:** Adjust the tension so it holds securely during the troll but releases cleanly when a fish hits. Too loose and your gear pops free in chop or current. Too tight and small fish cannot trip the release.

**Rod position:** Set the rod in a holder with a severe downward angle and the tip loaded into the release clip. When a fish strikes, the rod snaps up violently — this is the "pop" that signals a hookup. The visual drama is half the fun.

### Setting the Depth

Finding the right depth is the most critical variable in trolling for salmon. General guidelines:

- **King salmon:** 30 to 100 feet. Kings often suspend at the thermocline or along bait balls. Start at 50 feet and adjust based on sonar readings.
- **Silver salmon:** 20 to 60 feet. Silvers tend to be shallower and more surface-oriented than kings.
- **Pink salmon:** 10 to 30 feet. Near the surface, especially in the morning.
- **Lake trout (freshwater):** 40 to 120 feet in summer when they retreat to deep water.

**Reading the sonar:** Your depth sounder is your best friend. Look for:
- Bait balls (dense clouds of returns) — salmon feed below and around these
- Individual large marks below bait balls — these are likely salmon
- Thermocline (a band of denser water visible on some sounders) — salmon concentrate at and above this layer
- Structure edges — salmon patrol drop-offs and ledges

**Rule of thumb:** If you mark bait at 60 feet, set your downrigger at 50 feet. Salmon typically feed above the bait school, looking up.

### Trolling Speed

Speed is critical and species-dependent:

- **King salmon:** 1.8 to 2.5 mph. Slower is generally better. Kings are deliberate feeders and prefer a slower presentation.
- **Silver salmon:** 2.5 to 3.5 mph. Silvers like a faster, more erratic presentation.
- **Pink salmon:** 2.5 to 3.5 mph. Same speed range as silvers.

Measure speed at the downrigger ball, not at the surface. Current can create a significant difference between your GPS speed (speed over ground) and your actual speed through the water. A paddlewheel speed sensor or a downrigger with speed-at-ball capability is a major advantage.

**Adjusting for current:** When trolling with the current, your lure moves faster than the boat. Reduce engine RPM. When trolling against the current, the lure moves slower. Increase RPM. The goal is consistent lure action regardless of current direction.

## Lure Selection

### Herring

The universal salmon bait in Alaska. Whole herring rigged on a tandem hook setup (a "cut-plug" herring) produces a tight spinning action that drives salmon crazy.

**Rigging a cut-plug herring:**
1. Make an angled cut behind the gill plate, removing the head. The angle of the cut determines the spin direction and tightness.
2. Insert the front hook through the body cavity and out the side.
3. Insert the trailing hook into the tail section.
4. The herring should spin in a tight, consistent corkscrew pattern when trolled. Test it alongside the boat before deploying.

Fresh herring is vastly superior to frozen. Change herring every 30 to 60 minutes as it washes out and loses its action.

### Hoochies (Squid Imitations)

Soft plastic squid-imitation lures that are cheap, effective, and available in every color imaginable.

**Popular patterns:**
- Glow white and chartreuse — a consistent producer in all conditions
- Pink and white (the "bubblegum" pattern) — deadly on silvers
- Army truck (green and brown) — a king salmon classic
- Blue splatterback — good in clear water

Hoochies are almost always fished behind a flasher. Leader length between flasher and hoochie: 24 to 36 inches for silvers, 36 to 48 inches for kings. Shorter leaders produce more action, longer leaders produce a more subtle presentation.

### Spoons

Metal spoons trolled alone or behind a flasher are simple and effective.

**Popular spoons:**
- Coyote spoons (3.5 to 5.0) in cop car, gold/green, and silver/blue
- Apex spoons in hot pink, chartreuse, and pearl
- Diver spoons for shallow presentations

Spoons wobble and flash, imitating a wounded baitfish. They are easier to deploy than herring (no rigging required) and can be fished at higher speeds than cut-plug herring without washing out.

### Plugs

Diving plugs (Tomic, Luhr Jensen, Rapala) trolled on their own without flashers are effective for kings in particular. The plug's built-in action provides all the attraction needed.

## Flashers and Attractors

Flashers are oversized metal or plastic blades that rotate and flash in the water, attracting salmon from a distance and triggering strikes. They are the standard in Alaska salmon trolling.

**Types:**
- **Standard flasher** (11-inch): Pro-Troll, Luhr Jensen. The workhorse. Produces a wide, slow roll that transmits to the leader and lure behind it.
- **Dodger** (smaller, 7 to 8 inch): A more subtle action, useful in clear water or for pressured fish.
- **Rotators and chain swivels**: Some anglers add a chain swivel between the main line and the flasher to reduce line twist.

**Color selection:**
- Green and chartreuse — the all-around champions
- Glow white — excellent in low-light conditions
- Silver and blue — clear water and bright days
- Purple and black — overcast days and deeper water

**Pro-Troll E-Chip flashers** contain a small electromagnet that generates a faint electrical pulse mimicking a wounded baitfish. Whether or not this actually helps, they catch fish, and many Alaska charter captains swear by them.

## Mooching and Jigging as Supplements

Trolling covers water, but when you find a concentration of fish, switching to mooching or jigging can be more efficient. If you hook a fish while trolling, mark the location on your GPS and make multiple passes through the same area. If you keep getting hits in a small area, stop the boat and mooch or jig.

## Gear Configuration for Multiple Rods

In Alaska, the standard trolling setup uses 2 to 4 rods:

**Two downriggers (port and starboard):** Each carries one line. Set at different depths to search the water column. Example: port rigger at 40 feet, starboard rigger at 65 feet.

**Surface lines (optional):** Outriggers or flat lines fished near the surface with diving plugs or spoons. Effective for silvers and pinks, especially early and late in the day.

**The "stacker" rig:** Two lines on one downrigger, separated vertically by 10 to 20 feet using a stacker release. This doubles your coverage from each downrigger but requires careful handling to avoid tangles.

## Seasonal Patterns

**Spring (May-June):** Kings moving along the coast. Troll deeper (50 to 100 feet) near points of land and underwater structure. Focus on the king migration corridors.

**Summer (July-August):** Silvers and pinks arriving. Shallower trolling (20 to 50 feet) is more productive. Fish the tide rips and current lines where bait concentrates.

**Early morning and late evening:** The best trolling windows. Salmon feed most actively in low-light conditions. First light is the golden hour — be on the water and have gear in the water before the sun hits the water.

**Overcast days:** Often better than sunny days. Salmon move shallower when the light is diffused.

## Common Mistakes

1. **Trolling too fast for kings.** If your herring is washing out and spinning frantically, slow down. A proper herring roll is a slow, deliberate spin.
2. **Ignoring the sonar.** Trolling blind is a waste of fuel. Use your electronics to find bait and fish, then target those areas.
3. **Not changing gear.** If a pattern stops producing, change it. Switch colors, adjust depth, change lure types. Stubborn trolling is unproductive trolling.
4. **Running the same depth all day.** Salmon move up and down in the water column throughout the day based on light, tide, and bait movement. Adjust your downriggers every hour.
5. **Poor boat handling.** Avoid sharp turns that tangle gear. Make gradual turns and keep track of where all your lines are in the water.
